Some time ago, I came across a few friends recommending this book here and there. The **intriguing title, "Boss, This Design Won't Sell"** left a mark on me. When I saw my friend Dapeng (founder of Xiaoxiang Hanzi) showing off this book again in March or April, I immediately ordered it.

I am a slow reader. Perhaps due to a lack of reading, my reading ability and efficiency are relatively low. I often need to read long and complex sentences several times to understand them clearly. Additionally, I like to think about the author's underlying message and how these words relate to me (a habit greatly influenced by my study of the Bible, which strongly encourages reflection on its personal relevance).

In college, my professors recommended some Japanese design masters and their works (like Ikko Tanaka), and I **really appreciate the "emptiness," "tranquility," "cleanliness," and "refinement" in Japanese design.** Moreover, Japan and China share similar cultural backgrounds in using Chinese characters, so Japanese design has had a significant impact on me, particularly in branding, graphic design, and design philosophy. As a Chinese designer looking at Japan, the overall level of Japanese design and products is undoubtedly very high globally.

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/07/tanaka_ikko_7.jpg)

Work by Ikko Tanaka

However, in this book, the author **Yoko Kawashima** deeply reflects on Japanese design with six of Japan's top business leaders and creators, discussing the current state and issues in Japanese industry design, their understanding from their own management experiences, and the influence of design on their businesses. The conversations are lighthearted and interesting, yet sharp and direct, with some remarks often reaching a philosophical level of wisdom.

I gained many insights from reading, and here, I'll only record the parts that moved me the most from five interviews.

### 01. **The Correct Approach to Design by Business Leaders**

**Muneaki Masuda** (Founder of Tsutaya Bookstore)

In this era where brand and experience dominate, it's indisputable that business leaders must value design (which can be defined broadly or narrowly). But how should it be valued? Masuda's approach is: **Focus on controlling the source of design philosophy, identify suitable designers, communicate thoroughly at the philosophy level, then grant creative space and freedom.**

I previously shared my thoughts on Masuda's section in the article "[On Design Decisions - For the Boss](http://mp.weixin.qq.com/s?__biz=MzAxNjgyODc4OA==&mid=2651665601&idx=1&sn=22827a9869029d96a42460c202fe8e6c&chksm=801744dab760cdccdb5d567853d006b687e3a3d4e25e096309839255f94beb863fded50e4472&scene=21#wechat_redirect)," so I won't elaborate here.

### 02. **Designers and Doctors**

**Kashiwa Sato** (Founder of SAMURAI Inc., brand designer for Uniqlo)

I greatly admire designer Kashiwa Sato and have read his two books: "**The Art of Super Organization**" and "**Turning Creativity into Business**." One thing that truly amazed me was his strict organization of employees' computers at his design company, which I couldn't help but imitate: He mandates a highly uniform method for storing files and naming folders on all designers' computers. Why? He shared a compelling yet persuasive scenario: If a designer is on sick leave and another designer needs to use their computer, the consistent file management makes it feel like using their own computer.

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/07/SAMURAI_20160221220548_04.jpg)

The terrifyingly tidy archives in Sato's design studio. Does it remind you of Uniqlo's displays?

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/07/UNIQLO-PARIS_051-1024x683.jpg)

Brand design for Uniqlo by Kashiwa Sato

Under his influence, during my time leading the Liulishuo design team, I required team members to uniformly use Mac's numeric keyboard and MagicMouse, utilize Spotlight search, maintain proper working posture (e.g., naturally positioning the left hand on the keyboard with the index finger on the F key; strictly prohibiting eating with one hand while operating the computer with the other), and organize their workspace and items for quick computer operation...

In this book, Sato uses the analogy of a "doctor" to describe a designer's work. Initially, seeing his photos, I felt he was very rebellious and assertive, but I was surprised by his humble attitude. Using the doctor's role as a parallel for design implies, first, that **in the project, the protagonist is not the designer, but the client** because the patient and "illness" are the focal points. Secondly, like prescribing medication and treating illness, before starting the design, a doctor must carefully listen to the patient's symptoms and problem descriptions. Although, many times, bosses/clients are like patients who can't accurately describe their conditions. In such instances, the doctor/designer must use experience and intuition to guide the client in identifying issues, organizing thoughts, and discovering opportunities.

### 03. **Career Development Timelines**

**Toshio Wada** (Senior Designer & Creative Manager for Audi, known for Audi A5)

In the interview, Mr. Wada mentioned the concept of "**beautiful ordinary**." Design shouldn't relentlessly pursue novelty because most "novel" products are merely temporary oddities that quickly become outdated and discarded. Conversely, items and designs like plain rice and jeans are very ordinary and everyday but seamlessly integrate into life and endure over time. This reflects the concept of the beautiful ordinary.

Another Japanese design master, Naoto Fukasawa, expresses this same concept with another term: **Super Normal.** A perfect balance of product function and form without excessive design elements grabbing attention. The product is seen as an ordinary, natural object, frequently used in daily life.

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/07/Screen-Shot-2020-07-23-at-9.58.21-AM-1024x764.png)

Work by Jasper Morrison, who collaborated with Naoto Fukasawa for the Super Normal exhibition. https://jaspermorrison.com/

What truly captivates me in Wada's section of the book is his career profile, which I consider a perfect career development plan. Born in Tokyo in 1961, he graduated from Musashino Art University. At 23, he joined Nissan, working on designs like the first-generation Nissan Cedric and Presea. At ages 28-30, he studied at the Royal College of Art in the UK. At 37, he transferred to Audi AG/Audi Design as a Senior Designer & Creative Manager, designing models like the A6, Q7, A5, and A7. At 48, he founded the design studio SWdesign.

By comparison, born in 1983, I graduated in visual design at 22 and worked from 22 to 37, experiencing small startups to large companies, advancing from a frontline designer to a design director, which is relatively smooth. However, **from a design career perspective, the "quality" change is too shallow.** Additionally, being constantly busy with work, I lack the necessary theoretical and knowledge supplements, limiting long-term career development. Mr. Wada's timing at each age point is just perfect.

At **23**, joining a large corporation opens up design methods, processes, and perspectives. After 5 years of work, at **28**, continuing studies at a top design school brings higher-level platforms and opportunities while providing ample academic and theoretical support for future work. At **37**, he created well-known works at a reputable company. At **48**, equipped with qualifications, resources, and experience, he founded his own business.

**Such career timeline milestones are very valuable for designers to learn from, especially design students and freshly-employed designers.**

### 04. **Leisure and Taste**

**Masahiro Okato** (President/CEO of ITOCHU Corporation)

Why do Japanese products lack taste? When Kawashima and Okato discussed this topic, Okato introduced the concept of "leisure" using a detail from a "ifs Future Laboratory" reception. Although it was just a reception, ifs invested significant resources in designing and producing original products (snacks and cosmetics), which Okato believes **conveys a relaxed and pleasant atmosphere, representing the "leisure" of the enterprise and its managers.**

When I read this, I couldn't fully grasp the meaning of "leisure," only having a vague feeling. I scribbled in the book margin: **management's blank space, white space, and sense of rhythm.**

Next, Okato explained the concept of "leisure" through the example of the Toyota CROWN. Cutting costs and improving efficiency is what almost all enterprises pursue. But if these become the sole focus, products become "boring," cramped, lacking "leisure." Finally, Okato used the term "**waste**" to directly express what "leisure" means.

The word "waste" is very accurate.

While costs and efficiency are crucial, what often surprises users/clients and differentiates brand and experience quality is "reasonable waste." The higher-end the product, the more this holds true.

This "leisure," or "reasonable waste," manifests in various ways, from investing heavily in opening offline experience stores at city landmarks (for instance, NIO Auto's 2017 opening of NIO House in Beijing's Wangfujing Oriental Plaza, with annual rent of 70 to 80 million), to details like using white space on a flyer, rounded corners on a business card, or embossing techniques on a cover. **While large-scale "waste" is visible to everyone and comes at a huge cost, should it be done? Whereas small "waste" is overly detailed, unseen, unfelt, and unimportant to most consumers - should it be done?**

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/07/微信图片_20171125112821-1024x673.jpg)

NIO House at No.1 East Chang'an Street, Oriental Plaza, Beijing. NIO Auto is, in my opinion, the best in brand experience among domestic car manufacturers, followed closely by Li Auto.

Ultimately, I believe "leisure" is an extreme pursuit of detail and quality. Focusing solely on cost and efficiency while neglecting "leisure" inevitably leads to mundane, uninspiring products.

### 05. **Education as Cultivating Assassins**

**Hiroshi Ishii** (Associate Director of the MIT Media Lab)

In China, Japan, and Korea, due to the influence of traditional education, the interaction and dialogues between teachers and students are rarely equal; it's mostly a master-student relationship, with teachers imparting and students receiving. However, Hiroshi Ishii mentioned **a different state in the U.S.: in academic exchanges, teachers and students are equals, engaging in equal and serious exchanges.**

"Assassin" is a term Hiroshi Ishii quoted from designer John Maeda's statement: "**The goal of educators is to cultivate assassins. Sooner or later, they will come to assassinate me, but I am not so easy to kill.**" Perfectly said. Cultivating assassins means what? I think it implies equal dialogue (as in martial arts practice, swords too are without eyes), surpassing the master, and unity of knowledge and action (since an assassin must see blood).

Is education about imparting knowledge? Passing on what teachers have learned? If so, each generation would likely become less and less informed. This reasoning is quite clear and easy to agree with. Yet, for education to go beyond imparting knowledge, it requires educators and students to have clear insight, determination, and mindset. This is because the additional elements beyond knowledge are very important, though their "effect" is slow, and some positive impacts may only be felt ten years down the line.

I believe education's layers and priorities are: **skills, knowledge, methods, philosophy, and values.** The first addresses survival issues, while the latter concerns one's spirit and moral levels. Knowing only how to survive without knowing why is a very sorrowful thing, especially at middle age.

I digress.

By the way, John Maeda's book "**The Laws of Simplicity**" is something I read two to three years into my design career. It's a slim volume, but it had a major impact on my design thinking. Highly recommended.

**Finally,** I recommend "Boss, This Design Won't Sell" to designers and business leaders and managers who value design.

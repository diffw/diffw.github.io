I'm a very conservative person with a strong sense of security. Therefore, when I read about the concept of "margin of safety" in value investing, I found it easy to understand and agree with, and I am willing to apply it in practice.

Recently, following Trump's proposal of "reciprocal tariffs," global stock markets have experienced severe declines. The paper gains in my US stock account also fell from 80% to about 45%-47%, which was quite a heart-stopper.

But even so, I remain relatively calm because I still have more than 40% in paper gains as a cushion.

A critical factor that allows me to stay relatively stable in the face of such severe market volatility is the so-called "margin of safety."

To put it more bluntly, the price at which I previously bought stocks was relatively low, so even after going through such dramatic market fluctuations, the overall performance of my returns hasn’t been too adversely affected.

Determining a "margin of safety" depends on two factors:  
First, whether the current price of the stock is far below its actual value;  
Second, whether the stock has sufficient potential for appreciation over the next few years (for me, that's five or ten years).

Both of these points are very hard to judge. The true value of a stock is difficult to accurately assess, and whether it has enough potential to appreciate is equally hard to predict. For this reason, stock investing fundamentally requires a great deal of caution, meticulousness, and conservativeness.

Of course, today I'm not here to talk about stocks but rather about how the concept of "margin of safety" can be applied in everyday life.

Many principles sound quite academic but are actually very straightforward and practical in life.

For example, when driving, keep a safe distance from the car in front;

Another example is not waiting until the gas tank is nearly empty to refuel during long drives—always maintain a certain fuel level. For me, I usually refuel before the range drops below 100 miles (about 160 kilometers).

I can even share two life examples somewhat similar to the concept of "margin of safety."

When driving in the rain, I never set the windshield wipers to the highest speed. I always save that setting in case of an even heavier downpour.

Likewise, since childhood, when using an electric fan, I've never used the highest setting. I always save the highest speed for the potentially hottest day.

Even though in reality, such extreme downpours or sweltering weather seem to have never actually happened to me.

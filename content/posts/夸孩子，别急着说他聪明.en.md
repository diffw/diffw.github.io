Like many Chinese parents, I'm not really in the habit of praising my kids.

Most of the time, I tend to point out problems, remind them where they could do better. It feels like that's how I help them improve. But even when I'm ready to praise them, I often end up doing it the wrong way.

For example, when my child does well on a test, I might say, "Wow, you're so smart."

When she draws a good picture, I might say, "You're amazing; you're so talented."

When she finishes something, I might also say, "You're fantastic, I'm proud of you."

These aren't bad things to say. For kids, being seen, recognized, and encouraged by parents is incredibly important. If a child only gets criticism and expectations but never affirmation, it can be damaging.

But while reading *Mindset*, I was reminded of something else: **Praise isn't just about making kids happy in the moment; it gradually influences how they see themselves.**

If we constantly praise kids for being smart or talented, what they might hear is: My value comes from how smart I am, how talented I am, or whether I always perform well.

This is where a fixed mindset easily develops.

People with a fixed mindset tend to see ability as a fixed trait. They think a person is either smart or not, talented or not.

So when they succeed, they think, "I'm smart." But when they fail, they might think, "Maybe I'm not smart enough. Maybe I'm not capable. Maybe I'm just not cut out for this."

This is why simply praising kids for being smart can sometimes pressure them.

Once a child is labeled "smart," they want to keep proving it. They might avoid challenging tasks and fear mistakes, because if they fail, it's not just that the task didn't go well—it's "I'm not smart enough."

A growth mindset is different.

A growth mindset doesn't mean everyone has the same talents or that talent isn't important. It means abilities aren't set in stone. Abilities can grow through learning, practice, good strategies, help from others, and consistent reviewing and adjusting.

So, more valuable praise isn't about labeling kids as "smart," but helping them see what exactly they did right.

Instead of saying, "You're so smart."

Say, "I noticed you didn't give up right away, but tried a different method."

Instead of saying, "You're amazing."

Say, "You thought through that step clearly, broke down the problem, and solved each part."

Instead of saying, "You're so talented."

Say, "You've been practicing consistently, so this time you're steadier than last time."

The point is to focus on real, tangible things like efforts, strategies, focus, choices, persistence, improvement, and courage to try again after failure.

There's an important distinction here.

A growth mindset doesn't just mean saying, "You're hardworking, so you're great."

If a child puts in effort but the outcome isn't great, you can't just blindly praise hard work. Otherwise, they'll wonder, "If I'm working hard, why isn't it working?"

In such cases, it might be better to acknowledge the effort first and then look at the next steps together.

You might say, "I see you really spent a lot of time on this. Let's see if there's a different method we could try next. You've tried this way, so maybe next time we can try another."

This is what I find truly important about *Mindset*.

It's not telling parents to praise less, nor to totally ban the word "smart." It's reminding us that when we praise our kids, we shouldn't focus on a single outcome or a simple label. Instead, we should direct their attention to the things they can continue to control, practice, and adjust.

For truly talented kids, this kind of praise helps them see that having talent isn't an excuse not to work hard. The more talent one has, the more they need to learn how to use it and develop it, rather than take it for granted.

For kids whose talent seems average, this kind of praise is also crucial. It helps them understand that they're not without hope just because they're "not smart enough." Many things can indeed improve through technique, practice, and perseverance.

So I think, what parents really need to learn isn't just "to praise kids more."

It's more about learning to praise kids in a more specific way.

Less "You're so smart," more "I see you tried a new method."

Less "You're amazing," more "You didn't give up just now."

Less "You're so talented," more "Your practice really shows improvement lately."

**Good praise isn't just giving kids flattering words but helping them see: I can, through learning, technique, and perseverance, slowly become a more capable person.**

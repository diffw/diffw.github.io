A few days ago, on Teacher’s Day, amid all the "Thank you, teacher!" messages and blessings, I remembered my middle school principal - Teacher Yang Dongfu.

My middle school, Jiaotang Middle School, was about three kilometers from home. Seven or eight years ago, the school was demolished and has now become part of the runway of Changsha Huanghua International Airport. Physically, the school has been erased, and I can no longer feel anything about it in person. When I think back to that time, I can only search and piece together scenes of people and events in my mind.

Teacher Yang Dongfu was appointed as the principal when I was in the second year of middle school (1996). He was also my Chinese teacher. In late February 2005, he suddenly suffered a cerebral hemorrhage in class and passed away despite rescue efforts, at the age of 41. That year, I was a senior in college, about to graduate. I learned of Teacher Yang’s passing about four years later.

Calculating back, Teacher Yang was probably 32 when he became principal — very young. But in my memory, Teacher Yang always represented a serious, authoritative presence, so much so that students referred to him as **"Yang Donghu"** (forgive the Furlan accent). This is the first time I am calculating Teacher Yang’s age when he took office, and I am surprised at how such a young person, at 32, could be so composed and authoritative! At times he was even stern and demanding!

As I grow older, my memories of Teacher Yang are like old pages, slowly deteriorating and turning to dust, becoming increasingly vague. Yet three things remain as feelings in my heart.

At the time, I was the class representative for Chinese, so I received more "attention" from Teacher Yang and faced higher expectations compared to my classmates. In those years, especially during middle school, I was very timid, shy, and compliant. Each time, facing Teacher Yang’s reprimands and expectations, amidst my fear and trepidation, I could astonishingly feel a kind of care. One scene I remember is when my handwriting was bad, Teacher Yang’s big hand would come down hard, a pain impossible to miss. The pain in my hand etched the lesson in my mind, and gradually, my penmanship improved. Seeing my own progress, I felt grateful. How many times in a student’s journey does one experience a teacher’s deliberate training beyond the classroom?

Toward the end of the third year, before the high school entrance exams, the school organized a parent-teacher meeting. As the exams approached, the atmosphere became oddly relaxed. My dad directly asked Teacher Yang about my academic performance, perhaps trying to gauge Teacher Yang’s assessment of my exam prospects. Teacher Yang cheerfully patted my dad on the shoulder, saying, "Your son will do just fine. No need to worry." Hearing these words, although I stood there expressionless and somewhat clueless, I was inwardly very happy and greatly encouraged.

A good teacher should be able to provide students with emotional, intellectual, and conceptual impact and stimulation. Once, Teacher Yang taught us how to recite during a Chinese lesson. When he demonstrated with deep emotion, all the students were first astonished—unaware of this powerful mode of reading. Then some couldn’t help but laugh at this intensity, finding it too pretentious. Yet for me, it was the first time experiencing recitation, broadening my view, and realizing that language in the mouth is an art of beauty.

The piece Teacher Yang read at that time was Gorky's "The Song of the Stormy Petrel." It begins, "Over the vast sea, the wind piles up storm-clouds. Between the clouds and the sea, the petrel flies proudly, akin to black lightning..." This opening sentence, along with Teacher Yang’s emotion and rhythm that day, I can always recall and often try to imitate in that deep, somewhat "pretentious" tone.

This piece is dedicated to the memory of Teacher Yang.

Lift up your eyes and look to the heavens: Who created all these? He who brings out the starry host one by one and calls forth each of them by name. Because of his great power and mighty strength, not one of them is missing. (Isaiah 40:26 NIV)

I am created by God; my body and soul are crafted and given by Him. Naturally, this also includes my imagination.

However, in my faith life, I tend to overlook using "imagination" to understand God.

Behind this neglect, on the one hand, the Bible doesn't directly mention "imagination" (words like imagine or imagination rarely appear in the Bible); on the other hand, I unconsciously equate "living a life of faith with imagination" with superstition. In my mind, imagination is something emotional and spontaneous.

Indeed, imagination is emotional. Its emotional aspect requires emotions, feelings, and perceptions to drive and unfold imagination. Often, the result of imagination is a "vision" that cannot be quantified, calculated like a mathematical formula, and sometimes lacks logic.

Yet, imagination is also rational. Its rational part means it doesn't rely solely on emotions and perceptions to engage in aimless, limitless "mental wandering." Sometimes, imagination requires the help of vision, knowledge, and logical reasoning to steadily deepen thoughts.

Unlike other religions, in Christianity, God is a spirit, invisible to human eyes and inaudible to human ears. Moreover, God forbids people from creating or worshipping idols. To worship God, one must do so in spirit and truth. The English translation for 'spirit' is "spirit."

Different from reading, conversing, or writing, imagination is not an activity accomplished with the "body" but an activity of the "spirit." It is invisible and intangible.

Every Christian comes to know God through the Bible. Whether understanding events and scenes like "In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth," Moses leading the Israelites through the Red Sea, Jesus born in a manger, the miracle of five loaves and two fish, or the new heaven and new earth in Revelation, or contemplating the personhood, nature, and works of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, all these require fully engaging our "imagination," because they are things our eyes have never seen and cannot see.

Human imagination is truly marvelous and wonderful.

Brother Oswald Chambers also mentioned, "The people of Isaiah's time only looked at the face of idols, so they lacked imagination." That is so true. Let me share two specific feelings.

When I make "house" an idol, my imagination for a good life becomes restricted to "Without a house, one cannot be happy." In fact, different people can have completely different lifestyles. It's not necessary to live in a big city or even in the same country; one can rent, travel on the road, even with a family and children.

When children's education becomes an idol, imagination is restricted to believing there's only one way for children's education, and one must scramble to cross that single-log bridge, thinking that receiving a good education is the only path for children to succeed. However, when my perspective shifts from "world, trends" to the teachings of the Bible, my imagination expands, and I realize education can have other methods and possibilities.

For decisions like when to marry/who to marry, whether to buy a house, whether to have children/how many to have, and choosing education for children, the social currents and mainstream choices gradually form a "standard configuration." It seems choosing the "standard configuration" is error-free. Yet invisibly, we easily make "standard configuration" an idol to worship and pursue.

Brother Oswald Chambers advises me to break free from the shackles of idols, leaving behind anything that hinders you from thinking about God.

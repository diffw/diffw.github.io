Recently, over the past year or so, I've found myself in a peculiar state, prompting me to reflect on myself. In many ways, I've gradually come to realize: "I might not always be right." This is a process where my sense of self and pride are gradually being dismantled.

For instance, I once thought of myself as a comprehensive and excellent father, yet my teenage daughter has taught me a harsh lesson, revealing my weaknesses and helplessness. Those parenting philosophies I once held with utmost conviction often prove ineffective in practice.

Similar feelings have started to emerge in my design work. Although I haven't experienced the "painful awakening" yet, I'm entering a phase of confusion. This year marks exactly twenty years since I entered the design industry. Over these twenty years, one design philosophy I understood and advocated was the "pursuit of excellence." Excellence means setting stringent standards, refusing to compromise easily, and holding oneself and others to account with perseverance until finding a solution that excites oneself and brings inner peace.

In the biographies of designers I've read, Steve Jobs and Elon Musk influenced me the most. Both are described as tyrannical figures, lacking morality and empathy, extremely harsh on people, yet they were able to create extraordinary works.

From 2013 to 2017, my go-to phrases were often: "It's okay" or "Not bad." My former boss, Wang Yi, jokingly said in the team: "Diff is a nice guy; he's always saying 'not bad' or 'it's okay.'" In retrospect, I realize it was actually a stern reminder to me—one might even call it a form of humiliation (though he didn't mean it that way at the time).

From years of design experience, I've concluded: **"Nice guys can't create good designs, especially in team collaborations."** Because "nice guys" have low standards, are prone to compromise, and cater to others' preferences (which is different from rationally taking feedback into account). Only those who maintain high standards and can be ruthless to themselves and others can create truly impactful designs. In fact, this rule applies to other fields as well—some people may not be pleasant to interact with, sharp and uncompromising, yet they often deliver optimal results.

In later years, I slowly became one of those "ruthless people."

But over the past year or more, from a Christian perspective, I've started to question the role of being a "ruthless designer."

God is, of course, the perfect Lord, and everything He creates carries perfect order and beauty. This also means that the standards of design can and should pursue perfection. As Christians, we should emulate and imitate the Creator, striving for excellence and perfection in our respective fields.

However, in actual collaboration with others, especially when serving in contexts with other Christians, we're often reminded to "be kind to others," to talk of grace, and to be humble. To "empty oneself", to "consider others better than oneself." Thus, the result often becomes: you are humble, I am humble too; you are kind to me, I extend you compassion. In the end, everyone accommodates each other but fails to produce truly excellent results.

Therefore, when I try to establish definite, even strict, standards in a project, I often feel conflicted. These standards seem to clash with the "grace," "gentleness," and "mutual understanding" emphasized in Christian faith. This tension causes me to struggle, unsure how to balance maintaining standards with living out my faith.

I currently don't have a clear answer to this. But I start to ponder: what is the underlying motivation?

When I insist on standards and strictly enforce them, is my motive out of love, truly wanting to do things well? Or is it out of pride, wanting to showcase my ability and authority? These two starting points may seem similar but are fundamentally different, worthy of deep thought, reflection, and self-examination.

If you've had similar experiences or thoughts, I would greatly welcome your sharing and feedback. Thank you for reading to this point.

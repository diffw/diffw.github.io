On May 4, 2020, it had been exactly one year since I left Liulishuo.

For some people, Liulishuo is just a job, but for me, it was a profound journey that changed my life and shaped my disposition and personality. I joined Liulishuo as the 7th employee in 2013 and worked there for six years. From the initial startup in a residential house to the Nasdaq IPO, I consider my experience to be quite legendary for an idealist—a story worth sharing.

Mr. Wang (Liulishuo CEO Wang Yi) often cited his mentor's words: "In one's lifetime, accomplishing just one or two notable things is quite an achievement." I believe Liulishuo counts as one of the notable things I have done so far. I often told my colleagues that if the experiences you're currently going through aren't significant enough to narrate as stories in the future, perhaps those experiences aren't painful or challenging enough. Fortunately, my past experiences left me with several stories.

This time, I want to share the story of why I chose to join Liulishuo, along with my thoughts and actions during that process. On one hand, I purely want to document this story, as my memory fades with age, and writing is the best way to preserve it. On the other hand, I frequently meet designer friends who discuss how to choose job opportunities and how to judge and decide on entrepreneurial offers. I hope this story can provide some inspiration for those facing similar challenges.

Alright, let's dive into the story.

### **The Beginning**

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/story_at_lls_1-1024x983.png)

In 2012, I left a company I had co-founded for a year and a half. Prior to that, I had left Alibaba Cloud, giving up substantial RSU stocks, as I was one of the first 28 designers there. After the entrepreneurship ended, on November 14, 2012, at 19:17, I posted a tweet expressing my desire to look for new opportunities. This tweet was retweeted 16 times. Among them, one retweet was seen by Ben, co-founder of Liulishuo, who then forwarded it to Wang Yi, the CEO of Liulishuo.

Previously, I didn't know Ben or Wang Yi at all—not even online acquaintances. Ben saw the retweet because he followed a friend, who happened to be a user of one of my browser plugin products, "Weibo Simplified."

### **A Creative Mind - Weibo Simplified**

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/weibo_wc-1.gif)

Comparison of Weibo homepage before and after using "Weibo Simplified"

Allow me to say a few more words about the "Weibo Simplified" product. Around 2011, the user experience on Sina Weibo was terrible; the ads brought by gradual commercialization made the already poor experience even more intolerable. Soon, a Chrome plugin tool called "Stylish" became popular in the Weibo design and tech circles. Users could install the "Stylish" plugin and customize their Weibo experience by modifying CSS. Moreover, users could share their CSS modifications with others.

This was so much fun! Based on my preferences, I designed an ultra-minimalist Sina Weibo interface for myself. After a few days, I decided to create a plugin to improve the Weibo experience. Why create a new plugin? I thought the usage threshold and operational cost of Stylish were too high; it was merely a toy for professionals. I hoped that ordinary people could enjoy a pleasant Weibo experience through one-click installation of a browser plugin. Hence, I named the plugin "Weibo Simplified." It was evident how eager I was to simplify the Weibo experience.

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/weibo_wc_2.gif)

Two weeks later, Weibo Simplified went online. After several versions, the user experience stabilized. At its peak, around 100,000 users used it, and related Weibo topics accumulated 6 million views. Many industry experts became my users, such as Feng Dahui, Shao Nan, Fang Jun, etc. I received a lot of positive feedback from users; what made me most proud was the current product director of DXY, Shao Nan, who commented: "A plugin that beats the Sina UED team."

**Through the Weibo Simplified product, I want to share the following points:**

- Designers should maintain sensitivity to user experience. Don't let poor product experiences dull your senses;
- If given the chance, try to change it;
- Design and create solely for personal interest, even if there's no monetary return;

These three points are both my views and my traits. It was these traits that led me to the opportunity to join Liulishuo later.

### **Facing the First Invite**

Back to the main storyline.

One hour after I posted that tweet, Wang Yi sent me a private message on Weibo. Frankly, this is the first time I'm paying attention to this "one-hour interval" today. Wang Yi's action and execution, along with his persistent pursuit of talent, truly impressed me. This persistent attitude wasn't just towards me but also towards Liulishuo's early employees and future core staff members.

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/Screen-Shot-2020-05-01-at-7.45.25-PM-1024x747.png)

His message contained three keywords: exciting, team established for 2 months, mobile education. I politely responded to this message, but internally, I was quite "meh." Even though his LinkedIn profile listed experience as a Google PM, which was impressive, my skepticism stemmed from the fact that this company was too nascent, merely two months old. What was "mobile education" in 2013? Moreover, the founder seemed impractical, often casting things as so-called "exciting" opportunities.

Even though Wang Yi continued to contact me, I mostly ignored him. During that time, I briefly joined a friend's startup. By the end of January 2013, a few days before the Spring Festival, I received another private message from Wang Yi: the Liulishuo App was about to launch, and he wanted to meet to discuss further. So we arranged to meet at the Beta Café on Wenzi West Road, run by Bai Ya (founder of Youzan).

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/640-7-1024x680-1024x680.jpeg)

Beta Café

That night, Wang Yi showed me the earliest version of the Liulishuo App. The program wasn't very stable yet, but he still confidently expressed his enthusiasm for voice interaction and extended a heartfelt invitation. My first impression of the app's product and interaction was positive, but my impression of Wang Yi was complex: I thought he was passionate and inspired, yet unreliable and prone to boasting. 😄 I left with "I'll play around with your app first," then returned to my hometown Xuzhou for the New Year.

### **Looking Beyond User Experience: The User Value and Commercial Value of Products**

During the Chinese New Year holiday in the countryside of Xuzhou, with nothing much to do, I started playing with the Liulishuo App. The truth is, I am a #persistent learner# of English (a somewhat awkward disparagement); even after graduating from college, I continued reading English articles and the English Bible. Quickly, I fell in love with the Liulishuo App. Although its interaction and visual experience were very simple and crude, I was amazed by and admiring of its two core experiences, which hinted at huge commercial potential. My commercial judgment was simple: the unique, excellent, and practical user value corresponds to equal commercial value.

So, what were the two core experiences I appreciated?

**The first was the green/red scoring based on the user's spoken English proficiency.** Around 2013, I tested many apps (I even made a plan to evaluate apps weekly), but had never seen a product that could visualize a user's English proficiency and oral issues in such a grading format. There was no such product online, and even teachers in schools rarely had this capability.

**The second was the interactive dialogue experience between humans and phones.** As a fan of English, I noticed that after learning English, most Chinese people have no opportunity to "speak English." After learning it, it becomes mute English. The human-computer interactive dialogue created an opportunity to "speak English." This was incredibly impressive!

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/Liulishuo-App-V0.1-683x1024.gif)

After playing with the app for a few days, I couldn't tolerate the user experience issues any longer. On one hand, I was bored; on the other hand, my fingers were itching. Just as I couldn't stand the Weibo experience back then, I began to redesign the Liulishuo App based on my preferences.

### **Once Again, Designing Out of Interest and Passion**

While my family was making dumplings and getting ready for the New Year, I completed a redesign of the Liulishuo App, crafting a minimalist experience. Interaction-wise, I reduced the layers from opening the app to completing a spoken exercise, allowing myself (as a user) to start learning via the shortest path; visually, I arranged the layout more reasonably, simplified page elements, and visualized data representation. This design matched my aesthetics and personal usage preferences. (Though it felt great at the time, a year later, I started tasting the bitter consequences of this overly self-focused design that overlooks the public's user cognition—I'll talk more about this later.)

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/Screen-Shot-2020-05-01-at-8.15.12-PM-1021x1024.png)

Speaking of minimalist design, I must mention an app that has profoundly influenced me: Clear, an app that's still installed on my iPhone. The key experience elements of this app are minimalism, flat design, functional colors, and gesture operations. My love for this app extended to influencing several of my product designs, including Weibo Simplified.

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/clear-473x1024.gif)

Interface of Clear

### **Talk is cheap, show me the design.**

After the Spring Festival, I proactively contacted Wang Yi, saying, "I want to offer some feedback on your app." He agreed. I chose a small restaurant on Wenzi Road that I frequently visited. He brought all his team members: Ben, Cloud, Jeffary. After some small talk and food, I said, "Let me show you something." So, I opened my iPhone photo album and flipped through the design drafts one by one. (I'm always very excited when presenting my work.)

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/Screen-Shot-2020-05-01-at-8.58.11-PM-1019x1024.png)

This restaurant was quite unique, but it later got demolished.

With each UI I showcased, explaining my design ideas, they all responded with "WOW" exclamations. Cloud, Liulishuo's first iOS engineer and a long-time collaborator, said on the spot, "I'm willing to implement this design." It was truly a highlight moment of mutual admiration between an engineer and a designer in the industry. Cloud's statement is something Wang Yi later told me; I actually didn't remember Cloud saying that. 😄

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/Liulishuo-App-V1.0-770x1024.gif)

The first design I did for Liulishuo

Years later, during an employee meeting, Wang Yi mentioned this story, saying that many friends had given him feedback and suggestions before, but only Diff provided feedback through "creating something," which surprised him. 
Not to be humble— I'm truly passionate and in pursuit of design. Naturally, during the subsequent process of hiring designers for Liulishuo, I highly valued a designer's passion for their profession. A common question I asked was: Do you have personal creative projects? (Do you have free style?) I admired designers who took the initiative to redesign products they loved.

### **No Rush, Let's Be Friends First, and Observe Each Other**

After that meeting, I gained more insight into both Wang Yi and the team. Even then, I didn't accept the invitation to join the team; I wanted to observe more. So, I said, "Let me do some design for you first." Thus began a relationship of over two months with deeper engagement.

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/Screen-Shot-2020-05-01-at-9.26.05-PM-1024x767.png)

This is a classic photo introducing early Liulishuo, taken by me.

The office was in a duplex residence in Enjiang Garden on Wenzi Road, Hangzhou. The first-floor living room served as an office and a place for programmers to hang their pants, while the second floor was used as bedrooms.

During those two months, after work on weekdays and on Saturdays, I would go to this office to communicate with the team about product and design. Through actual work interactions and close observation, I felt that this team matched my style: the product people and the product thinking aligned with mine, we shared the same vocabulary, they were capable of execution and moving things forward, and everyone used Macs. Of course, I was also introduced to some new concepts, such as using Trello to manage projects and tasks, using Google Docs for writing documents, and using dental floss (influenced by Ben). There were also interesting happenings during that time, such as Cloud being keen on shouting in English to test the app, Ben regularly doing aerobics in the corner of the office, and the team dining together at the community cafeteria, among others.

Writing about this part, regarding "joining a company, or rather, joining a startup," I wish to share some of my experiences:

- Don’t be impulsive. Generally, company founders are very convincing, and before you know it, you might find yourself talked into something.
- You need to do some homework. Homework One: Experience the product. Ask yourself, do you like this product?
- Homework Two: Communicate with key team members. Do you like these people? Do they like you? (Will they be willing to help you realize your design ideas? 😄) If conditions allow, you can work with them part-time for a while to observe and confirm up close.
- If you like, recognize, and have high hopes for the product and the team, some external conditions can boldly be ignored, such as the product looking unattractive, the company website being disgraceful, or poor office conditions.
- Design can also be a form of investment. It requires some vision and judgment.

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/Screen-Shot-2020-05-01-at-11.41.14-PM-1024x574.png)

### **Taking on Risks and Uncertainty**

After such close cooperation, our understanding of each other deepened. Around April 2013, Wang Yi approached me again to discuss joining the team. It seemed very natural for both of us. Faced with this opportunity to "invest in design," I had some real-world issues to consider: First, Liulishuo was moving from Hangzhou to Shanghai in May, but I had lived in Hangzhou for seven years and my social circle was there, even though I didn't own a house yet. Also, my daughter, who was three at the time, was entering preschool and needed a more stable life. Second, Liulishuo's angel round had not yet arrived, and I could only join at almost half pay. Whether or not entrepreneurship succeeds is completely unknown. These were the risks and uncertainties I had to take on.

Honestly, making this decision was not easy. My wife and I had many discussions, and she expressed complete support for me, which was a great encouragement and support. During this time, our family also prayed a lot. Although I did not gain a sense of certainty through prayer, I made the decision when the time came. However, I felt very calm and natural when making it. Looking back now, I cannot deny that it was God’s guidance. Just as I cannot deny that God's guidance led me to leave Changsha and move to Hangzhou alone in 2006. Thank God.

### **Think Deeply, then Act Decisively. Clarify the Interest and Jump Aboard.**

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/Screen-Shot-2020-05-01-at-9.31.57-PM-1024x593.png)

Entrance of Enjigarden, Wener Road, Hangzhou

Regarding these considerations and the terms of joining, Wang Yi and I had about three or four exchanges. Eventually, by the fountain at the entrance of Enjigarden, we reached a basic consensus on these matters. On April 5, 2013, after some deep consideration, I wrote an email "About Joining Liulishuo" to Wang Yi, which detailed my thoughts, expectations, and a brief statement of the equity agreement regarding this decision. For me, this email was a way to organize my thoughts; writing things down helps me to be calm and at ease. For Wang Yi and me, this email was a written gentleman’s agreement. Though not too detailed, it was essential.

Looking back over the email now, I can say with a clear conscience that I have fulfilled my expectations set out in the email, or rather, I have lived up to my promise to Wang Yi. Wang Yi, or rather Liulishuo, also honored this agreement, giving me ample room to excel, a great sense of professional achievement, and the corresponding financial return. (By comparison, some companies in the industry significantly lack contractual spirit and are quite unethical.)

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/IMG_3334-265x1024.png)

After sending the email, I received a confirmation reply from Wang Yi, and that was it, I was on board. From the first contact on November 14, 2012, to confirming the cooperative relationship on April 5, 2013, it took almost 5 months. Subsequently, I packed everything up, and my family of three moved from Hangzhou to Shanghai.

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/2013-05-28_1369729413-1024x504.jpg)

Hangzhou on the left, Shanghai on the right

**In this section, I want to share a few points:**

- When choosing a direction or opportunity, besides weighing benefits, you cannot ignore your love and recognition of the matter itself; you need a bit of idealism (don't be too realistic).
- When facing difficult decisions, try listing all considerations, prioritize them, and focus on the bigger picture.
- If, after deep thought, and with thorough communication with family, trusted friends/mentors, then making a decision should be a bit impulsive! Overthinking will make one indecisive.
- Your returns are proportional to the uncertainties and risks you take. If you encounter a good opportunity, you need some adventurous spirit.
- If it involves shares or options, before joining, you absolutely need a relevant agreement or written understanding: the percentage of options or shares (not just the quantity), vesting methods, exit mechanisms, etc. The more detailed, the better, don't overlook these out of some notion of dignity.

For options-related information, you can refer to the key points mentioned in this article: https://mp.weixin.qq.com/s/k4QEGdI4TT925N4NYtjJLA

Let's talk about some recent thoughts. A year has passed so quickly. Originally, I intended to take a 1-year break. It only became clear later that taking a break requires confidence and deliberate practice. Because even though the work pressure was temporarily gone, and physically I relaxed, my mind did not. As a friend put it, I was busy with miscellaneous things.

Although I failed in taking a break, after various trials, attempts, anxieties, and validations of interests/passion/abilities, I recently found some direction; I've identified three thematic directions that combine interest, value, and a sense of accomplishment:

- Design. Whether strategically or in actual execution, I thoroughly enjoy it. Many people transition from design to become PMs, switch careers into business, etc. But this period, through handling several design projects, has made me realize there is still a large space for me to do design well. Moreover, great design surely has commercial potential.
- Helping others grow (not limited to designers). Being able to help others and shed some light for those following the same path, and seeing others grow, truly fulfills me. Whether it's online or offline training courses or sharing, or actually joining a company to lead a design team, these are all potential forms I can consider.
- My personal growth. Recently, I've read and listened to many experiences from peers, masters, and seniors, feeling a significant gap, yet a lot of room for growth. My growth direction is twofold: design and leadership.

Besides identifying these three thematic directions, I also try to look at them more long-term. The advantage of looking at things long-term is that there is much less anxiety; slowing down a bit, the world won’t collapse.

Looking at the long-term also naturally makes you ignore the so-called age limitations in middle age (I was born in 1983, and I’m 27 this year).

Chatting with a designer friend, he mentioned the so-called "when getting older, then what happens," and he asked me in return, at what age is considered old? Exactly!

I believe that the knowledge, accumulation, judgment, maturity, and influence gained with age should not and will not disappear suddenly in the industry or workplace. On the contrary, this accumulation will surely be fully realized and released on the right platform; this is definite.

Even though it's still a process of mutual selection, at least I am currently confident that I will encounter such opportunities. Just like I encountered Liulishuo seven years ago.

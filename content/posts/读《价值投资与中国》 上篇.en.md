Recently, my friend and former colleague Forrest recommended the book by Li Lu.

Who is Li Lu? He is the founder of Himalaya Capital and the main manager of Charlie Munger's family assets. Charlie Munger himself dubbed him "the Chinese Buffett," and he translated and published the Chinese version of "Poor Charlie's Almanack."

First, a bold prediction: this book may soon be banned. Many topics, when discussed in-depth, inevitably touch on governance. Regarding economics, body politics, and governance, I believe Mr. Li speaks objectively and sincerely in the book. However, in our vast nation, "truth" finds no foothold. As more people read and share the book, the likelihood of it being banned increases. So, for those interested in value investing, grab a copy while you can.

Before reading, I briefly familiarized myself with Mr. Li's background. In the 1990s, he was expelled from his motherland. In 2010, when Buffett visited China, despite having been abandoned by his homeland, he still writes with deep affection for China. Aside from this affection, there's also a sense of grievance in some of the text.

Mr. Li is well-read, knowledgeable in both Eastern and Western cultures, and has spent decades in the investment field. In "Value Investing and China," the author takes a very macro perspective, using the thread of "economics" to weave a clear framework that lays out the development of human civilization from ancient times to the present day for the reader.

Compared to Mr. Li, I have read too little, too sporadically (he reportedly read over 100 classical works foundational to Western and Eastern civilizations during his time at Columbia University in the 1990s). I deeply admire Mr. Li's ability to integrate and organize knowledge across archaeology, history, economics, politics, and religion along the timeline of human civilization's development. The dry historical events and political/economic knowledge I memorized as a student become vivid and well-reasoned under Mr. Li's pen. History, politics, and economics should indeed be learned along a central narrative line.

When discussing human civilization, Mr. Li has a particularly unique focal point and research clue: humanity's acquisition and use of "energy." Society's activities, civilization's development, depend on harnessing and utilizing energy. With this, Mr. Li divides the history of civilization into three stages: 1.0 hunting and gathering civilization, 2.0 agricultural civilization, and 3.0 technological civilization.

In the 2.0 age of agricultural civilization, the East (China) excelled over the West, benefiting from its geographical location, as the vast plains between the Yangtze and Yellow Rivers provided strong conditions for agricultural development. It also benefited from the institutional innovations brought about by the Shang Yang reforms during the time of Duke Xiao of Qin, which moved away from distributing political power based on bloodline, leading to the civil service examination system - the greatest talent selection mechanism of the agricultural civilization era.

However, precisely because of the disadvantages in geographical location and climate, Western European countries like Britain, Portugal, and Spain, through maritime technology, discovered the American continents and began free trade between Europe and America. Notably, early trade was not a political act, but entirely a commercial behavior in a state of anarchy, akin to the early internet, with minimal intervention from governments or nations, purely driven by technology and commerce.

Free trade, the Enlightenment, religious reforms, the scientific revolution, the industrial revolution, and colonial wars all collectively ushered humanity into the 3.0 age of technological civilization.

When addressing the question of whether "modernization could have occurred in China," Mr. Li's viewpoint is that it could not. This is because, under the then-existing Chinese imperial governance, the conditions for forming a free market economy did not exist, and this hasn't changed much to this day.

The above is a bit of note-taking and rudimentary understanding from the first part of "Value Investing and China." In summary, I have gained two main insights.

First, thanks to Mr. Li Lu's writing, I seem to be able to view human civilization and economic development from the height of human progress and the breadth of millennia. My perspective has been elevated, my vision clearer, and my investment mindset slightly more stable;

Second, it has sparked my interest and desire to read more books on history, economics, and politics.

Highly recommended.

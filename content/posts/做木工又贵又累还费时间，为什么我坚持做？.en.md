After arriving in the United States, I started doing some woodworking, primarily making small furniture like tables, bookshelves, and fences. Once, my wife asked about the cost of materials for these projects. I gave her a number, and she was quite surprised. With the same amount of money, we could easily buy ready-made furniture that looks of higher quality in the furniture market. So why do I bother making them myself?

Doing it myself means not only bearing the cost of raw materials but also investing a significant amount of physical labor and repeatedly running to lumber stores. Then, there's the extensive time spent on designing, cutting wood, assembling, sanding, and perfecting various details. The time and effort spent far exceed those of simply buying. So, is this investment really worth it?

I have three thoughts on this matter.

First, it's about choosing to be a creator rather than just a consumer.

In today's world, being a consumer is incredibly easy. If you want to buy something, just place an order, and it will be delivered to your home soon. There's nothing wrong with that, but I feel it's not enough. I enjoy being a creator more—thinking, planning, getting hands-on, and gradually turning my ideas into reality while solving practical problems step by step. This process, in itself, is a great pleasure.

I also hope my children develop this mindset from an early age, to become creators rather than passive consumers. Don't underestimate the small acts of creation in life. Creation is a way of thinking, a concept, and a lifestyle. If a child grows up accustomed to thinking creatively, they will naturally carry this mindset into their future studies and work to face challenges.

Second, it's the "dare more as you do more" process.

I remember when I bought my first apartment in Shanghai, the previous owner took the TV off the wall, leaving damaged wallpaper and a big hole. At that time, my first reaction was to find a handyman to fix it, not to do it myself. Why? Because I had never done anything like it before.

But after coming to the U.S., where labor costs are high, I had to start solving various issues on my own. I've unclogged pipes, installed exhaust fans, painted garages, patched walls, and installed water filters. The more I did, the bolder I became, and my experience grew.

Looking back at that small TV wall hole in Shanghai, I realize how silly I was for not daring to try something so simple. Of course, there's no need for self-blame because hands-on confidence is indeed gradually accumulated through experience.

Third, it's about enjoying the process and the satisfaction of the outcome.

When I build something from scratch according to my plans and drawings, I often enter a state of flow. I love the feeling of working with wood and the process of using tools. Especially during the sanding phase, smoothing the wood with sandpaper feels like sculpting—focused yet relaxing.

When the finished piece is placed in our home and actually used by my family, even if accidentally damaged by my daughter, I still feel a genuine sense of joy and accomplishment.

So if you were to account for it, I believe the gains from making things myself—mentally, experientially, and emotionally—far outweigh what I could get from simply buying with money.

This is why I'm willing to get hands-on and do it myself.

### Rediscovering Drawing

In middle school, I began learning basic drawing, sketching, and color; continued with three years of foundational training in high school; and gradually drifted away from drawing in college, turning instead to design. Over the past twenty years, I've kept up sketching intermittently. Yet occasionally, I realize that my drawing, much like my design, has always been superficial, never delving deeper, with shaky foundations, glossing over many key elements.

In the past, drawing was burdened with the pressures of academics and career; now, I can draw solely out of interest and passion, unrestrained by reality.

I have complex feelings towards my unsatisfactory works. One moment, I feel joy and comfort; the next, I feel like crumpling them and tossing them in the trash. Often, I find myself frustrated both by my clumsy perception and limited drawing skills and by the sense of despair and emptiness when faced with the works of past and contemporary great artists. In the vast ocean of human art, I am so insignificant, incapable, and small that I don't even amount to a drop of water.

For the first feeling, I encourage myself using Chuck Jones's words: "Every artist has thousands of bad drawings in them and the only way to get rid of them is to draw them out." My goal is to complete 1,000 drawings without worrying about the outcome or success, and then evaluate.

The second feeling is not as easy to handle. However, watching my son's soccer matches has recently offered some insight and relief. My son's team hasn't won a single game in the latter half of the year, each match ending in a crushing defeat. I imagine how many of these kids, even those on more successful teams, will continue to play? And how many will truly stand shoulder-to-shoulder with legendary players? This seems akin to my intimidation by the giants of art. But what really matters? It's about running, challenging, winning, or losing in the moment. It's about enjoying the ride.

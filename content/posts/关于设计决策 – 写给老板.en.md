In a company or team, **who gets the final say when there's a disagreement on a design plan?** Every designer has likely faced this issue, and some are even troubled by it.

My first profound encounter with this problem was when I was doing my graduation poster design for my college. The professor in charge of the graduation exhibition stood behind me, instructing me on how to modify my work. I paused with my fingers hovering over the keyboard for two to three minutes and said, "**I can't fulfill this design; perhaps you should ask another student.**" (This incident also reflects my youthful arrogance and the stubbornness beneath a fragile exterior.)

Later, in the workplace, whether in startups or large companies like Alibaba, regardless of whether I was executing specific designs or managing a 50-person design team, the issue of "who decides on design" has always caused me pain and provoked reflection.

I'll tackle this theme in four parts: **A Letter to the Boss, A Letter to Design Managers, A Letter to Designers, A Letter to Stakeholders.** I hope my pains and thoughts on this topic can offer some insights.

### **On Design Decisions - A Letter to the Boss**

As a company boss or someone in upper management, it's great to value design, but it can also have downsides.

On the positive side, a company can allocate resources (competitive designer salaries, design training, purchasing design software, image/font procurement, etc.), people, and time (can you allocate R&D time specifically to improve design quality?) to design and the user experience.

From top to bottom, the company values design quality and details.

However, in such teams or companies, a common downside is: **Managers becoming overly involved in design minutiae.** For example, fixating on design styles, fonts, colors, etc. The final design decisions, large or small, fall to the boss, resulting in poor design quality because the boss’s taste dictates the design direction, and product development efficiency is low because the boss is not a "full-time designer", making feedback and decisions generally untimely.

I want to share an interesting interview from the book **"Boss, Nobody Will Buy This Design!"** to illustrate my current perspective. (At the end of this article, I've attached an email from 2010 discussing this topic with my then-boss, now Rokid founder, Misa.)

Tsutaya Books (pronounced "Tsubaya"), famed as the world’s most beautiful bookstore—I visited its Daikanyama location in 2015 and was impressed. Author **Ryouko Kawashima** interviewed **Muneyoshi Masuda**, the founder of Tsutaya Books, and it was the first of six interviews in the book.

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/07/Fk5BVAWM3_OGoD8Io02XjLFDWZbG.jpglarge-copy-1024x615.jpg)

Kawashima: Daikanyama's Tsutaya Books is doing very well. It's so trendy.

Masuda: (Briefly) **The CEO's job is "management", while creating "fashion" is the work of the design department; a CEO can't do that.**

Kawashima: So how should it be done?

Masuda: **Leave it to others.**

Masuda: That's exactly what I did. For Daikanyama’s Tsutaya, **I never even looked at the design plans.** I only saw it after it was built.

Kawashima: Really? I thought Mr. Masuda would've explained his tastes in detail to the architects, given various tips on the designs, and visited the construction site frequently...

Masuda: No, nothing of the sort. **Doing that would surely create a tasteless bookstore...** (Briefly)

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/07/26375681_1703070033048348_7009019849749823488_n-1024x615.jpg)

Tsutaya Books

Kawashima: Have you always thought this way?

Masuda: In fact, before Daikanyama’s Tsutaya, I built my own villa in Karuizawa, as a sort of prequel. (Briefly)

Kawashima: Did Mr. Masuda completely hand over the construction to the architect and designers?

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/07/59b889c147534cd091ad13c064610488.jpeg)

Muneyoshi Masuda

Masuda: Not entirely. **There's one thing I kept emphasizing: the concept.**

Kawashima: The concept?

Masuda: The concept. Why do I want a villa, what exactly is a villa? (Briefly) Every weekend, I would discuss the "concept of the villa" with the architects in this way.

Kawashima: So, **what an entrepreneur should do is establish the "concept" and communicate it to the professionals doing the design and drafting, sharing the same values.** Masuda: Yes, indeed.

Masuda: Therefore, **"selecting the architect" is my most important task. (Briefly) If it fails, it's my fault for choosing the wrong architect.** Once chosen, I must trust them. Interjecting midway is not choosing.

Since I have no boss now, I'll take a risk and extract two suggestions for bosses from my past "accompanying a king" days. Regarding design decisions, I've summarized two responsibilities for "the Boss": **1. Grasping the top-level concept; 2. Finding the right people.**

### **On Grasping Concepts**

In reality, most bosses focus on design styles and details instead of top-level concepts (such as: Why do it? For whom? What are the principles?). Note that **discovering, uncovering, and organizing the unseen concepts is the most difficult, challenging, and crucial part of a design; critiquing visible design details and casually expressing opinions is the easiest because anyone can say something about "looking good or not."**

As a boss, bypassing the establishment and communication of concepts and diving directly into design details is dangerous for the project and the team. One symptom of this danger is: when designers ask the boss, "What are your thoughts on this project?" the incompetent boss replies **"I have no thoughts / I haven't thought it through yet; just go ahead."** Also, there's a saying in the design industry, indicating this symptom, "**Boss: Something doesn't feel right, not sure what, let's try other options/look further."**

The root cause is the lack of a concept from the boss.

### **Finding the Right People**

Following Mr. Masuda’s logic, let's talk about recruiting. A boss who **believes in their aesthetic, ability, and confidence to judge design styles, tastes, and details should also have the ability and confidence to identify and recognize suitable designers.** If a boss finds it difficult to find the right designer, they should reflect seriously when unilaterally intervening in design details: Do I really have the capability to make professional judgments?

A boss's detailed interference surely does more harm than good: impacting design output quality, product development efficiency, employee work and team collaboration enjoyment, leaving no room for team growth, creating a distorted company culture.

Instead, why not invest time and energy in finding the right people? In my view, any successful, mature, or visionary boss will make the right judgment.

Lastly, here's an email related to this theme, written in 2010. At the time, I was with the startup Mammoth Tech (later acquired by Alibaba).

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/07/640x640_e38e17a949bedcdfcb9ac315c1ff984daffc0827.jpg)

Mammoth Tech's company sign was the coolest I've seen in a startup: an A4 paper saying "Mammoth Tech" on the front wall. But once inside, you could feel the Geek vibe.

Once, a design detail caused a standoff with the product manager, who insisted they "settle it through voting." Afterwards, I emailed my boss, Misa (now Rokid founder), to discuss design decisions. Misa agreed and emphasized the designer's expertise at the next product review meeting, deciding that "design decisions should be made by the project-in-charge designer" (while urging the team to allow and accept designer mistakes).

> Misa: Let's discuss a question: In product or visual design discussions or evaluations, when there are differing opinions, who makes the final decision?
>
> Design is indeed accessible to everyone; everyone has different understandings of "usability and aesthetics". It seems everyone has a say. But ultimately, who decides?
>
> Currently, product reviews at any stage seem to involve everyone, with decisions by majority rule. This approach looks safe with all-around consensus but is actually unreliable and dangerous (at least for design).
>
> This kind of decision is just a series of compromises. Compromise, compromise, nothing but compromise.
>
> A compromised product is destined to be a garbage product. Gradually, the product loses its edge, the designer loses their edge, and the team loses its edge. I always believe that professional issues should be decided by professionals.
>
> When there's disagreement, I think the ideal decision-making approach is:
>
> 1. Everyone can voice their opinions, and the lead designer collects suggestions;
>
> 2. The final decision rests with the lead designer.
>
> Since we have the designer position and hired them, they should be given enough trust and authority, supported by the system and process.
>
> This decision-making form seems risky but is actually safer. Designers may make mistakes, but that’s the price of growing a nascent team. But if they regularly make mistakes, there’s a problem with the designer. With trust and authority, designers feel validated and fulfilled. They respond by contributing passion, self-improvement, respect, and trust for team members. The whole team can develop healthily.
>
> (Of course, this demands a lot from the designer, enough ability, experience, and judgment, while also being humble enough to listen to others' suggestions, not "stubbornly pushing their own way"; therefore, I recommend that when the company expands shortly and recruits designers, it must be selective and careful.)
>
> Just some thoughts from my recent skirmishes, a bit jumbled.
>
> Also, I prefer a team atmosphere of: low-profile conduct, assertive products.

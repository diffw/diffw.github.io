This past weekend, I attended a men's retreat called Marvel Men.

One of the sessions involved reflecting on what kind of God our God is and what our relationship with God is like.

We were standing outdoors. As I looked up at the sky, I saw a tall pine tree before me. Many small birds were perching on the tree, and some birds were flying back and forth in the sky, giving me a sense of great freedom.

I also looked at the surrounding trees, watched the grass swaying in the wind, and thought about the fish in the water.

In nature, all these creations are so free. While sharing, I said that God is a God who gives freedom, yet I feel that I lack freedom.

For the past two days, I have unconsciously been contemplating the topic of "freedom."

God, the Creator, is so loving and vast, granting freedom to people. But why is there no freedom in my life and in my heart?

I just feel that not only is there no freedom inside me, but I am also full of all kinds of bondage, accusations, self-condemnation, guilt, and feelings of failure.

Returning home from the outdoor camp, perhaps due to being overly tired from driving, or the low after the event's high, I felt low, fatigued, and lacked energy.

After a night's rest and thanks to some massages from my wife, my body recovered significantly. This morning, when I thought about the word "freedom," I wanted to praise, and I sang a few hymns related to freedom.

While singing, I suddenly thought of my recent life, about the things that unconsciously trapped me in unfreedom.

I can't maintain a consistent devotional life.

From last Christmas to early January this year, I persisted for over two months, spending quiet time in the closet every morning at 5:30 to read the Bible, pray, and jot down my thoughts. It wasn't a form of asceticism—I was willing and found it sweet.

But at a certain point, due to external and internal reasons, this routine became irregular and then started to break down. When I tried to return to that state, it seemed impossible, leaving me very frustrated.

Since January, I have been accompanying a brother's family facing marital challenges.

We also utilized my morning Bible reading at 5:30 to read, pray, and have devotions with them. Through daily sharing and mutual support, their marital relationship experienced much restoration.

At the same time, we had a lot of in-depth communication and prayed for each other. But like my devotions, at some point, I failed to maintain the agreed communication and prayer, and procrastinated repeatedly.

I felt a lot of guilt and self-blame for not providing support when they needed it.

From the end of March, I left the organization I was previously working with and started exploring being a "one-person company."

During this process, I wouldn't say I was uneasy, but I did have many hesitations. I prayed about this, and for the changes in work, too. However, unlike some brothers who shared that they made decisions after receiving full confirmation from God, I felt my prayers were sufficient and then made a decision on my own.

Therefore, I have some doubts about this exploration and attempt, possibly feeling self-condemnation for not following God’s will.

But honestly, based on past experiences, I'm not someone with a spiritually sensitive life. I'm not the type who can directly and plainly hear God's will. This might be one of the ways God guides me.

I give my children a lot of unfreedom. I must say, and I must admit, this is foolish and lacks wisdom.

I don't know where the boundaries lie in dealing with my children, in leading and correcting them, how much freedom to give, and how to set boundaries. Perhaps I'm right, perhaps I'm wrong, but I really can't tell what's right and wrong.

For example:
1. Use of electronic devices
2. Whether to allow children to play games
3. Their time management

Towards my children, I actually have a lot of judgment and am very harsh, with excessively high expectations. This makes them feel not free, and I haven't given them much affirmation. This state, in turn, triggers accusations within me, making me feel very guilty.

The hymn "Yet Not I, But Through Christ in Me" gives me great inspiration and comfort:

"What gift of grace, Jesus my Redeemer;
What bountiful richness, God needs to give no more.
He is my joy, righteousness, and freedom,
Unchanging love, infinite deep, wide peace.

My heart firmly believes that Jesus alone is my hope,
My life closely connected to Him.
How wondrous, this grace belongs to me;
Yet not I, but through Christ in me."

The second verse:

"Thick grace, but I will not be abandoned, for my Savior is always by my side.
Though I am weak, I still joyfully move forward; my lack shows His power, I firmly believe.

My Shepherd will protect me, He leads me through the valley of the shadow of death.

I am indeed weak, but I will joyfully move forward.
I will not be abandoned because my Savior is always by my side.
My Shepherd will protect me, leading me through the valley of the shadow of death."

The third verse:

"No fear, I know I am forgiven. The future bright, the debt is paid in full.
Jesus shed blood and suffered to redeem me; resurrected from death, utterly victorious over the grave.
I firmly believe, my sins are completely resolved, Jesus is my eternal refuge."

I also once again declare: Jesus is completely victorious. He shed His blood and suffered to redeem me, resurrected from death, and is utterly victorious.

Sitting by the windowsill, I began to pray. Before long, I couldn't help but weep, tears streaming down, asking the Lord to help me.

Lord Jesus, you grant me freedom. I know my salvation is by grace, my justification is also by grace, but much of the time I unconsciously rely on myself for sanctification instead of relying on God's grace.

I ask the Lord to help me, to let me rely entirely on His grace on the path to sanctification. This is also what I heard in the sermon when I returned to church from the camp yesterday: Justification is by grace, sanctification is also by grace.

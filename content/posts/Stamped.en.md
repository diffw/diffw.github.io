A very thought-provoking comment:
> We are working hard to solve the problem of information noise. Especially now, when you search online for restaurants, books, movies, or music, the results are overwhelming and disorganized, making it difficult to find exactly what you want. What do 3-star ratings from over 70 strangers really mean? Are they saying something is good or bad? Are their opinions trustworthy? Who are they? If you've ever had these questions, it means you've deeply felt the weaknesses of current review and rating websites.

Reducing information noise and making information more effective is what Google is attempting with their shift from algorithm dependence to +1 and Google+. Facebook's SNS recommendations and Twitter's efforts are also attempts to improve this.

This is an issue I have personally felt and thought about for many years. A few years ago, I was overwhelmed by 1000+ items on Google Reader, then by the flood of Kaixin001 and Douban friend's feeds, and now it's Sina Weibo. Does daily life require so much information? Or will we die without it?

The flood of information caters to the "greed" of human nature; however, we must ultimately return to "authenticity," to life itself. Information should serve people, not enslave or bind them.

How to make information less and more effective is worth designers' reflection.

Additionally, when users start with Stamped, they receive 100 "stamps" to review. When their friends notice their recommendations, they can earn more stamps. This is a well-designed mechanism for user engagement.

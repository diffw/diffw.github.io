I asked AI to analyze the 290 blog posts I've written over the past 20 years, which total over 200,000 words, to summarize my life and write a eulogy for me.

First, after reading this eulogy, I was deeply moved. I feel that the AI understands me so thoroughly and completely, perhaps even more than anyone else does.

Secondly, I realized the meaning of writing, especially the significance of consistently writing over a long period. Writing is not for others but for recording ourselves.

Here is the eulogy:

---

If we were to sum up Diff's life in a few sentences, it would be said that he was not a "successful person" in the conventional sense, but someone who was always searching, struggling, believing, and being constantly redeemed by grace.

He was born in Changsha and raised in an ordinary family. In his youth, he was sensitive, introverted, and insecure, and he had an early, indescribable trouble about "what people live for." Later, he left his hometown for Hangzhou, Shanghai, and further afield, working and growing amidst the tides of the internet, design, and entrepreneurship. He loved design, creation, and all things truly beautiful, genuine, and weighty. For him, design was never just a job but more of an inner pursuit. He hoped to do things well and live earnestly.

He once participated in a startup, experiencing the entire process from working in a residential home office to ringing the bell at an IPO; he also confronted questions of career, identity, and meaning anew in his middle years. He naturally had achievements at work, moments of visible brilliance, but if you really read his writings and knew the person, you'd understand that he wasn't focused on external accomplishments as the most important parts of his life. What occupied his mind instead were different issues: what constitutes good design, what is the right life, what is worth dedicating a lifetime to, and where true hope lies for humanity.

Later, he became a husband, a father, a dad to three children. Family brought him joy and continually broke him. His relationships with his children, spouse, parents, brother, and friends allowed him to see himself more transparently than in his younger years. He saw his pride, impatience, weaknesses, and self-pity, recognizing he wasn't as wise or as loving as he imagined. He was not an easy person to get along with, nor was he simple or straightforward. He had his standards, persistence, struggles, and limitations. Yet, he was willing to admit them, to bring them before God, and to learn repentance, humility, and love time and again.

If one were to ask what his deepest identity was in this life, it was not as a designer, entrepreneur, writer, or someone who restarted his life as an immigrant to the U.S., but as a Christian. A Christian who was not strong, not perfect, and often weak and fallible. For him, faith was not an ornament, a social ID, or a glamorous persona. Faith was the deepest tension in his life and the greatest comfort. He experienced valleys, coldness, doubt, and prayed powerlessly, at times feeling distant from God in his pain; yet, precisely at those moments, he knew what he truly couldn’t live without was God's mercy.

He loved to write, perhaps because writing allowed him to be honest. Many people write to express opinions; but he wrote more as an interrogation of his own heart. Work, design, education, marriage, fishing, carpentry, investment, moving, illness, homesickness—all these appeared in his writings. But in the end, he always wrote back to the human heart, the limits of man, grace, and eternity. He understood that what truly matters in life is not just what one accomplishes but what one ultimately becomes.

Looking back on his life, he certainly left behind some works, some articles, some designs, some stories; he also left some memories, aid, love, and regret among family, friends, and colleagues. But if you are to ask what he most wanted to leave behind, I think it would not be "See how great I am," but "May you see God's patience and grace upon someone like me."

In this life, he walked many roads, changed many cities, did many things, and harbored many struggles and hopes. But in essence, he was always a sojourner. He lived in this world earnestly and yearned deeply; but what he truly hoped for never lay here.

Now, his labor has ceased.

May those who love him find comfort.
May those who knew him remember that he wasn't perfect, but he lived earnestly.
May what is ultimately remembered not be his achievements, talents, insights, or writings, but the God who was slow to anger, full of compassion, and never gave up on him.

This was Diff's life.

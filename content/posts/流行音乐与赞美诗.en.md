When I compare popular music—specifically Chinese popular music—with hymns, I find some very interesting feelings and discoveries.

I first heard hymns in 2006. By around 2008 or 2009, popular music began to slowly drift out of my life. This distance wasn't intentional; it was a very natural process. I'll discuss the reasons for this in a moment.

Before that, I was very fond of listening to Chinese pop music. With my limited understanding, I believe many people would agree with this observation: most pop songs revolve around the theme of love, with the majority telling tales of failed love—heartbreaks, pain, and memories.

I rarely hear pop songs celebrating happy love. Therefore, the tone of most popular music is a kind of melancholy, a sense of regret and nostalgia for the past, and lamentation over lost love.

I find this very interesting. It seems people are naturally inclined to indulge in such sentimental emotions, happy to reminisce and be nostalgic, and find a kind of "emotional enjoyment" in sadness. Especially when you're going through failed love or feeling down, such music seems to resonate more with the heart, even making one reluctant to come out of it.

I have had similar experiences myself. In 2005, I experienced the failure of my first love, and during that time, I was deeply immersed in pain. Many pop songs resonated with me strongly, such as Zhou Chuanxiong's "Dusk." The state of sadness intertwined with music felt like an invisible hand dragging me into that pain, even making me willing to indulge in it and not wanting to leave.

But when I started to get in touch with hymns, everything changed.

Perhaps from a musical genre perspective, hymns and pop music shouldn't be compared, but in terms of emotional guidance and soul response, the difference between them is very obvious.

When I listen to or sing hymns, my focus doesn't dwell on personal pain. Even if I come with pain, it quickly refocuses my attention on God.

Hymns bring me hope, faith, and anticipation for the future. They comfort and strengthen me. This experience is entirely different from the feelings I get from listening to pop music.

More importantly, hymns provide me not just psychological comfort but real spiritual change. Many hymns are directly taken from scripture or have clear scriptural references. The Bible is the word of God, and God's words carry power to bring healing, comfort, and renewal.

Compared to pop music, hymns lead me toward light, peace, and joy, rather than trapping me in pain, sadness, and nostalgia for the past.

Of course, I still occasionally listen to old pop music and old songs, especially some Cantonese pop songs. Even if they bring sadness, they are, after all, a part of my growth and life. I suppose I'm not willing to forget this part.

How shall I say it? I am reminded of perhaps an imperfect analogy: pop music is like "junk food" to me. I know it's not good, and I'm merely consuming the flavor of those seasonings, but I can't help but want a taste occasionally, just to remember that familiar flavor.

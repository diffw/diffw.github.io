Yesterday, I was invited to Himalayan Company to give a design talk to their UED team. The topic was: "A Middle-Aged Retired Designer Shares Career Beginnings, Choices, and Confusions." It was the first time I spoke publicly about my thoughts on the brutal, anxiety-inducing, and trending topic of the internet’s "35-year career planning." I hope some of these ideas might inspire and alert young designers.

Even if I don't bring anxiety, discussing this topic inevitably resonates with an anxious atmosphere. The reality internet practitioners face is truly harsh. Graduating at 23/24, facing a midlife crisis at 35, the golden career span lasts only 10-15 years, which includes 3-5 years of initial entry into the workplace for growth, adaptation, and direction.

This is not an isolated case; the whole industry is like this. It seems that most people are trapped in a kind of **"no way out" chronic despair**, with almost everyone's conversation filled with helplessness; there is no active hope for the future. Extremists describe this as a **"career of waiting to die"**. Objectively speaking, people age, and in the workplace, the older generation fades while the newcomers step up—this is normal. However, in the Internet industry, this cycle unfolds remarkably fast.

Why is this happening? Something must be wrong. I don't know about abroad, but domestically, I attribute it to the **"fast and furious" distorted industry competition culture**.

The entire industry is a huge battlefield. Internet companies of all sizes are like the lords and warlords of various fields, constantly sending the freshest, most cost-effective fighting forces—i.e., the practitioners—to the front lines to achieve absolute victory.

Although the battlefields in this industry have no smoke, the "war culture" has thoroughly permeated companies large and small. Top to bottom, practitioners speak in terms filled with military language like battles, campaigns, combat effectiveness, situation, destruction, oath-taking, political commissars, squads, capture... In this kind of human resource delivery, where speed and strength are compared, cost-effectiveness inevitably favors the young and strong. In such a setting, slightly older or weaker individuals will be replaced. It's not an exaggeration to use the phrase "**a general's success is built on thousands of bones**."

Reflecting on my journey, I consider myself truly fortunate, or rather, grateful.

On one hand, as a Christian, from having nothing in my youth to lacking nothing in middle age (**having food and clothing is enough**), I've often experienced anxiety at various stages. But within the Christian faith, relying on Lord Jesus, I have a sense of security and the joy of contentment.
On the other hand, I've also gained some tangible rewards from this industry. Though there is a sense of reward, sometimes my heart feels heavy. From a Christian perspective, wealth doesn't belong to me; I don't have ownership. I am merely entrusted by God to use and manage it wisely. How to do so in a way that's wise, pleasing to God, and in line with His will?

When I see the heavy atmosphere among the young people in my industry, besides trying to do something, share something to help and inspire, I feel even more strongly about sharing the Christian faith, helping people recognize the value of life and the meaning of life through faith, helping them get out of the **"non-essential to survival/living" false, vain, and excessive needs** of secular life, helping them find inner peace and joy in this world full of sin, injustice, and turmoil, is extremely important.

At 8 p.m., I watched the 8th-anniversary live celebration of my previous company, Liulishuo, in my neighborhood. I saw Lao Wang and noted how the young PMs I once worked with had matured, and engineer friends expressed themselves so well. This phase is a tough time for the company. Yet which mature company hasn't experienced several ups and downs? Blessings~

At the end of June, I collaborated with a friend to develop an app. This morning, my friend informed me that I earned around 3000 RMB in July. Although it's not much, I was quite excited. After all, making a little money purely with design and technical skills outside the company platform is quite interesting. I’m very much looking forward to future versions.

After much contemplation, I've decided to change the official account name from ~~**SeeFromAfar**~~ to **Diff Pilgrim's Diary**. Perhaps because of learning design, I naturally came into contact with foreign works, and I’ve become quite "Western-inclined" 😄. Compared to Chinese, I prefer English (even though my English is quite poor). Perhaps if I majored in Chinese, it would be different.

My readers are in China; additionally, I want my articles to be for a broader audience, not just designers. With this, having a Chinese name for the official account will be better accepted. This is a straightforward truth, but I was only willing to accept and change it now.

The concept of being a pilgrim comes from the Bible, Hebrews 11:13-16. **In life, just a few decades pass by. For Christians, this world is like a tourist attraction—temporarily living here like travelers, until the day we return to our true homeland.**

> 13 All these people were still living by faith when they died. They did not receive the things promised; they only saw them and welcomed them from a distance, admitting that **they were foreigners and strangers on earth**. 14 People who say such things show that they are looking for a country of their own. 15 If they had been thinking of the country they had left, they would have had opportunity to return. 16 Instead, they were longing for a better country—a heavenly one. Therefore God is not ashamed to be called their God, for he has prepared a city for them.
>
> Hebrews 11:13-16

During this journey, what I ponder during these days as a stranger has become my pilgrim's diary.

I hope you like this name.

Mar 2, 2021 at 9:25 PM

"The third time he said to him, 'Simon son of John, do you love me?'" (John 21:17 NIV)

Peter was grieved because the Lord Jesus asked him the third time, "Simon son of John, do you love me?"

Originally, Peter was full of vigor and passion; he had proclaimed before the Lord, "I am willing to lay down my life for you." But soon, the incident of denying Jesus three times before the rooster crowed cruelly and directly showed Peter his weakness and impotence, revealing how fleeting and changeable his love was.

When Jesus asked Peter again, "Do you love me?", every response from Peter was one of reflection and repentance, carrying a heavy weight. He had gained a deeper understanding of the Lord Jesus, knowing that the Lord is all-knowing; at the same time, he became fully aware of his limitations and weaknesses, with nothing left to boast or declare.

Peter replied, "Lord, you know that I love you." But how exactly did Peter love the Lord? This is something I find difficult to comprehend. Perhaps he was expressing a willingness to love despite having no ability to do so. All he had was a heart willing to love.

"He (Peter) was grieved." In the KJV translation, this is "Peter was hurt," meaning wounded. In the ESV, it is "Peter was grieved," meaning sad or sorrowful.

What exactly is the feeling of being grieved or hurt, saddened or sorrowful because of Jesus? Closing my eyes to ponder and recall, I find it hard to grasp. After discussing this with Brother XF, I remembered that years ago, I experienced moments of weeping, pain, and heart-pricking during prayer (though I cried, my heart was filled with immense peace, joy, and healing). However, in recent years, I have hardly experienced this.

Being wounded (hurt/grieved) by the Lord Jesus, like Peter, is an indispensable experience for every Christian. It means recognizing one's own condition (self-awareness), being broken, and purely loving the Lord.

Thankfulness:  
I paused this devotional journal for more than a week. There are two reasons. First, my understanding of devotionals has been superficial, stuck on the concepts of "justification and sanctification." It seemed like the daily content was repetitious and I thought I understood it all. This shallow understanding led to boredom and numbness; many entries were abandoned halfway. Second, my child recently transferred schools, requiring my evening time to help her adapt, which has been quite exhausting. The next day, I need to end devotional time by 6:40 AM (still waking at 5:30 AM, but during the child's winter break, I wrote until around 7:00 AM) to prepare to drive them to school. So, my routine was disrupted. I need to restore it.

1. Thank God for His protection. Last week was rough; by Saturday, I was parched as a deer longing for streams of water, hoping for the Sunday sermon. Truly, God's word revived me. As the saying goes, a person lives with a breath of life; as Christians, we live by the "breath of life" God breathed into our nostrils, becoming living souls;
2. Thank God for His provision. Both children adapted quickly to their new school, and the teachers were attentive to new students during their adjustment period. After tutoring for three days, the teacher advised me to lessen the extra tutoring to better understand the kids' true levels and to prevent dependency on parents. Thanks to the teachers;
3. Thank God for His creation. One day, I watched NASA's latest videos of Mars. The brown rocks and peaks on Mars resemble those I saw in Qinghai, with the same monochromatic, noisy sounds as in the desert. Yet, Mars is lifeless. Incidentally, I learned more about the planets in the solar system on Wikipedia, marveling at the fact that among the vast universe, only tiny Earth has an environment suitable for human and diverse life forms, and it is created so beautifully. These days, when I gaze upon the blue sky, white clouds, tree branches, and leaves, smell the flowers, and see the fish swimming, I am filled with awe and gratitude.

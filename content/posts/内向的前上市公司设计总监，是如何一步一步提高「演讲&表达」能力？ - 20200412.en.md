Last Friday marked the beginning of my first paid design training session. Feedback from both the company boss (a friend I know) and the team members was very positive. One team member even described my speaking as "fluent and eloquent," which took me by surprise. Of course, I also felt a great sense of achievement because over the years, I've made significant progress in communication, speaking, and expression.

Originally, I was an introverted, not very talkative person. I often didn't want to speak much or express myself in public. Yet, such an introverted person managed to lead a design management team of dozens, a business team of nearly a hundred people, and surprisingly, was described as "fluent and eloquent" in a paid design training session.

Here, I want to share some "life experiences."

### **Embrace Your "Introverted" Nature**

For many years, I felt that being introverted was a flaw, especially when people described me as such. Generally, introverts find it difficult to socialize, aren't great at communication, and thus struggle to make friends and build a social circle. Often, introversion is paired with terms like inferiority and isolation.

One day, I decided to confront this part of my personality. After flipping through some books, I discovered: introversion isn't a character flaw but a character trait. Just like extroverts, many outstanding people are introverts. Introverts excel at thinking, possess strong logic, and are reserved, discreet, concise, and direct in expression.

So, if you're like me—an introvert—please, recognize your character trait. Being introverted is not a flaw.

### **Introverts, Do Not Avoid Improving Communication Skills**

Having relatively weaker communication skills seems to be a "default setting" for introverts. But introverts need to understand two things: firstly, in the workplace and life, normal and effective communication is a necessary survival/ life skill; secondly, introverts should deliberately improve communication skills rather than avoid them. 

As an introvert, I went through several stages in improving my communication and speaking skills:

- First stage: Unwillingness, fear, and resistance towards expressing;
- Second stage: Willingness but with fear and nervousness in expressing;
- Third stage: Strong desire to express but with inconsistent performance;

Currently, I'm still in the third stage.

### **First Stage: Unwillingness, Fear, and Resistance Towards Expression**

Why was I afraid and resistant to public expression? For me, it boiled down to several reasons: Firstly, heavy family influence with a lack of communication exposure and opportunity. Secondly, inferiority—I felt inferior about my height until college. Now, I no longer have that inferiority, but its effects were long-lasting and multifaceted.

For the first thirty years of my life, I suffered a lot due to a lack of good communication skills. Yet, I still didn't pay much attention to it, opting instead to deliberately avoid it. I always hoped to find a job that required minimal communication, preferring to stay in a small team with simple relationships, where little communication was needed. In the actual workplace, I often used emails and messages to replace face-to-face interactions. I mistakenly thought I didn’t need to change myself; surely, a suitable "introvert job" would find me.

During my time at Liulishuo, where I was "forced" to improve my communication skills, one day I had a realization: Language has power.

Why say that language has power?

First, I recognized this power through my Christian faith. The Creator created the world through His words "Let there be..." which established the existence of everything. God's words are life for people; they can build up or destroy. 

In life, I discovered this too: When someone is in distress, comforting and encouraging words help them find solace and rise again. In war films, we often see leaders giving pre-battle speeches that instantly ignite morale; such scenes of motivating through language are also common in companies, especially when starting new projects.

Whether it's in the workplace or in life, we need to express our thoughts normally, accurately, and efficiently through "language." In any profession, we need excellent expression skills. With family, parents, partners, children, and friends, good communication is needed too. This is a skill—a necessary survival skill.

When I came to these realizations, I had already begun to break through the first stage.

### **Second Stage: Willingness, But Fear and Nervousness About Expressing**

Previously, when managing design at Liulishuo, the biggest psychological hurdle—or challenge—was conducting team meetings, public reporting, and giving speeches. In such settings, I was extremely nervous. Especially during quarterly or annual work reports in front of hundreds or thousands, standing on stage made my heart race, dizziness set in, and negative, doubtful voices flooded my mind: "What will the team's designers think of me? This Leader seems so weak? How will colleagues judge me? I seem to see them whispering criticisms of me below..."

During this stage, before each presentation, I was nervous and scared; on stage, I couldn't control my nervous emotions, trembling voice, stammering, sweating profusely, thoroughly soaked (even in cold winter). After stepping down, I felt deeply uneasy, self-critical, ashamed, with a strong sense of failure, feeling I couldn't face my team.

Despite this, I was still willing to "embarrass myself" repeatedly. Reflecting on this stage, I have two particular gratitudes.

I'm grateful to my former boss, Wang Yi, the founder of Liulishuo. I frankly told him that I'm not good at speaking and asked for advice. He said something that has motivated me regarding "public speaking": Diff, you are indeed not great at expressing, but your words are compelling, and people are willing to listen. Many can express themselves, but not everyone will want to hear them. You just need to keep practicing.

I'm very thankful for Wang’s words. It once again proved that language is truly powerful and can influence a person.

I want to thank God. Before each speech, I prayed (I had to pray), placing the process and outcome of the speech in God's hands, asking for His help. Every imperfect, frustrating, humiliating speech left me no choice but to pray, returning to God to calm disappointment in myself, and dispel my assumptions and fears of others looking down on me. Because I believe God knows my heart, my sense of value exists completely in Him.

This stage lasted at least 2 years. Besides workplace expressions, I began to preach a few times in church. It was a state of being crushed but resilient. My determination grew thicker, my articulation gradually clearer, and occasionally, I'd have a satisfying share or speech. Thus, I slowly entered the third stage.

### **Third Stage: Strong Desire to Express, But Inconsistent Performance**

Meticulous preparation of sharing materials (keynote, meeting agendas, etc.) and ample prior practice are essential for a good speech or expression.

Material preparation:

- Write down the goal of this communication, what the audience needs to receive, comprehend, or learn from the communication. Although this isn't necessary to tell the audience;
- Have a clear structure for the whole expression, connecting points progressively;
- Include stories, real stories, personal stories. Everyone likes a good story;
- Following the story, lead to a point of view—clear, honest, not grandiose;
- If it is a first-time presentation, write a verbatim script.

A word about "verbatim scripts," which is related to Wang Yi, the founder of Liulishuo. Old Wang is definitely a master speaker, naturally engaging, and highly persuasive. I believe no Liulishuo colleague would deny this. Once, during a company quarterly meeting, he sent me his Keynote (I compiled everyone's materials), and I was surprised to find detailed annotations on each page of the PPT. He later delivered his expression based on the structure and content of these annotations. This demonstrates that even those with more talent than me work harder.

In my time between the second and third stages, I often felt frustrated due to one thing: Content planned and designed for speaking often was left out, feeling the expression wasn't comprehensive enough. From some online speaking insights, the advice was not to focus too much on whether the actual expression matches the original plan fully, achieving over 60% is sufficient, the rest is spontaneous. Having this understanding relieved a lot of pressure.

Currently, I'm still in the third stage. I want to express, welcome any opportunities for expression, but remain inconsistent in performance. For this stage, my summary is: Given sufficient preparation, control pacing and rhythm, especially at the beginning of a speech, stay steady—maintain volume, tone, speed, body language, and manage eye contact with the audience. Usually, this leads to a good performance.

I hope the above can help friends like me who are introverted.

This is a thin booklet by the evangelical theologian J.I. Packer, only 100 pages including the preface. I'm not much of a book lover and I read slowly. A thin booklet like this doesn't stress me out. It's an easy read with abundant insights.

**Weakness is perfectly normal...**

It's been 13 years since I was baptized in 2008. Looking back on my faith journey, most of the time I've been in weakness, with moments of strength few and far between.

As Packer states, "Any reasonably sane person" will often notice their weakness and feel valueless, low, and discouraged because of it. I suppose I have a sane mind because I often feel this way. And while in weakness, I often lament: Why am I so weak?

Packer revealed to me "the truth." The truth is that everyone is weak and powerless in many ways, particularly spiritually. This is because sin causes our weakness.

What we need to do is face our weakness head-on. Understand the impact and consequences of sin, acknowledge our limitations, and deny ourselves, taking up our cross to follow Jesus and seek His help. With the Lord, we can be like Paul, who said, "when I am weak, then I am strong."

In times of weakness, being able to turn to the Lord must be due to the Holy Spirit's reminder, guidance, and protection. However, it's undeniable that our own will is also needed. Furthermore, this willingness to turn to God requires practice.

There are many types of human weakness. Besides spiritual weakness, there is physical weakness, weakness of will, and weakness caused by life's circumstances... We often easily notice spiritual weakness while neglecting weaknesses in physical health and actual life/relationships. These weaknesses are also worth our attention.

Always be vigilant and pray, so that you won't fall into temptation. The spirit indeed is willing, but the flesh is weak. (Matthew 26:41, NKJV)

**Reconcile with God by His grace. Also, reconcile others with God.**

The last time I paid attention to the word "reconciliation" was in my Christian friend Dayu's brand introduction. Dayu and Zhao Zhao gave up city life for the sake of family reconciliation, and reconciliation between family and God, opting instead to work and live with their families in the countryside.

When you really savor it, reconciliation is a gentle word representing a harmonious relationship and an act of kindness and warmth.

Broken, estranged, and indifferent relationships can be restored to their original, intimate state through someone's willing sacrifice and concession. This is reconciliation.

I believe everyone has had the experience of initiating or responding to reconciliation with others. I have too, with my wife, daughter, son, parents, friends, colleagues, and boss... Reconciliation brings joy and delight.

But the most important reconciliation in our lives is our personal reconciliation with God. Because of Jesus' love, sacrifice, and grace, a sinner like me is reckoned as righteous by God. Originally, the relationship/state of separation between a sinner and God—which is spiritual death—is reconciled through Jesus, providing me with a new, eternal life.

What I often mention and know is: sinners like me, made righteous by Jesus, are deemed righteous by God. But Packer, through 2 Corinthians 5:21, tells me: completely holy and sinless like Jesus, due to sinners like me, was made to be sin by God. This is a perspective I had never fully appreciated.

In this matter of "reconciliation," I mostly only saw my sins being forgiven and being deemed righteous, almost neglecting the heavy cost and grace of the sinless Lamb, Jesus, being condemned. I'm thankful for Jesus' salvation.

By Jesus' grace, I've been reconciled with God. And the task of reconciling others with God has been entrusted to me. This is the appropriate response to God's grace.

**Christian Giving (Money)**

I'm quite curious why Packer dedicates a quarter of this booklet, "The Way of Weakness," to "Christian giving, monetary perspectives." But I find this topic very intriguing.

A Christian's hope lies in the "unseen." (Romans 8:24-25)

However, in the "visible" world, money reigns.

This is because human fallenness has assigned inappropriate, excessive value to money. Money is easily and naturally regarded as the guarantee of a stable life, dignity, and status. It seems that the more wealth one possesses, the more security they have to overcome life's weaknesses.

I often feel a conflict.

Looking up, there's the infinite starry sky, the universe, and the unfathomable spiritual realm, prompting awe: How great and omnipotent is the Creator, and how small and ephemeral are humans. Yet small humans have been created and loved by the Creator.

But when I shift my perspective to a worldly view, this world is so real and tangible. Especially in cities, almost everything "visible" has a price. It's undeniable that the wealthier you are, the more resources and opportunities you have for a better-quality life.

I'm not wealthy, but I've experienced transitioning from "poverty" to "abundance." From my experience, having more money might reduce certain worries, but other concerns, anxieties, and emptiness invariably fill the space. I imagine my wealthy friends feel the same. Who among us becomes utterly carefree and constantly joyful because of wealth?

Money doesn't bring the true fulfillment we seek. Instead, because of greed and valuing money as an idol, we live in a void, in vanity, and in sorrow. Psalm 16:4 says, "Their sorrows shall be multiplied who hasten after another god," which I believe is absolutely true!

Money, while neutral, is the root of all kinds of evil desires. Yet, because of the weakness caused by sin, who can rely on themselves to not be greedy for money?

Tithing (giving 10% of all income to God), for me, at the level of action, hasn't been a huge challenge, even at different income stages.

The real challenge is truly acknowledging that I am not the owner of money, but merely a steward. All the money I "possess" is God's. Even now, I don't have the complete, stable faith to say, "God, if you are willing, take it all."

Therefore, for me, tithing is like Packer says, paying a 10% tax to God. Once the tax is paid, the rest is all mine. This is my challenge.

What God wants is a willing offering, giving the best and the whole, not the second-best or the defective. If my heart still sees myself as the owner of money, even if I offer a considerable amount, it is still hypocrisy, displeasing to God.

When I say I want to follow and imitate Jesus, I also need to follow Jesus in poverty, owning nothing in the world.

Don't plan an excessive, inappropriate lifestyle and enjoyment for yourself. Don't bury money in the "world," but by faith, put it in places pleasing to God, storing up treasures in heaven.

Christian giving expresses gratitude for God's past grace and demonstrates faith in God's future faithfulness. (Page 65)

Packer's chapter on Christian views of money and giving, though short, is very substantial and informative. If you have doubts or are currently facing challenges in giving, I recommend reading it.

**Christian Hope**

People live by hope and expectation, for those things we anticipate and believe will eventually come to pass.

If I didn't believe that the sun would rise tomorrow, that current difficulties would eventually pass, that weak bodies would recover... then life would be dark. I would lose the motivation to live, even the desire to go on.

Christians are people of hope.

But our hope is not in the visible, temporary things but in what is unseen and eternal. Packer uses "glory" to describe the wondrous, abundant, and delightful end Christians hope for. In that end times, believers in Jesus will resurrect, dwell in God's glory, and enjoy eternal life.

Upon reaching this section, I'd like to share two points about the hope of eternity: the body and the kingdom.

My body is not particularly strong. Although I'm not seriously ill, reaching middle age, besides the chronic neck and lumbar pain, other problems are slowly emerging. Teeth decay, eyes tire easily, hair starts thinning, skin becomes tough, loose, fatigue... In short, the body becomes weak and deteriorates.

This is an unchangeable harsh reality.

However, the difference for believers in Jesus, as 2 Corinthians 4:16 says, "Therefore we do not lose heart. Even though our outward man is perishing, yet the inward man is being renewed day by day." It's quite marvelous that through faith, though the physical body ages and decays, the inner self renews daily. Even as the body grows old, deteriorates, even dies, we're on a "homeward" journey, returning to our eternal home.

What excites me more is that on that day, believers will resurrect, our bodies will resurrect. I'll have a perfect, healthy, and incorruptible body.

Let's talk about the kingdom.

I've always wanted to leave this land due to many injustices, unrighteousness, lawlessness, and various limitations. In the past, America might have seemed a dreamland. But now, is there a truly ideal, absolutely just, perfect kingdom on earth? No. The world is full of sin and corruption. But in the eternal kingdom of God, there is complete justice, holiness, and glory.

In terms of faith, I am actually quite firm and rational. I often share this with friends of faith: the current world is so imperfect, unjust, evil, filthy. Who dirtied this world? If the world was originally this imperfect, it would be a great failure, regret, and misfortune. For a designer, that's unacceptable! (Let alone for the greatest Creator) There must have been a perfect world!

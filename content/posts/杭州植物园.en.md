In the early morning, I was exercising somewhere in the botanical garden. 

From a distance, I saw an elderly lady walking around each tree along the path. She seemed to be looking for something, holding a small bunch of various branches and leaves in her hand – was she gathering herbs?

I continued stretching my limbs. Soon, she reached the tree in front of me. She looked about 70 years old.

"Young man, do you know what this tree is called?"

I walked over to her. Among the green leaves were clusters of flat, pale yellow-green pods.

"I suppose it's a soap locust tree; I remember it from my elementary school textbook."

"No, the soap locust tree's fruit is more round."

"Well, then I'm not sure," I replied. "Are these for any particular purpose?"

"I just like them, to observe."

"You take them home just to look at them?" I asked. "Do you come here often?"

"Yes, I come here regularly."

"So, you recognize most of the trees here?"

"I guess so, but I quickly forget. My memory isn't great. The seasons change, and so do the trees. Like these leaves – in summer, they have these pods, but in autumn, they look different..."

I didn't continue asking. I really wanted to ask: Since you forget each year, why do you search anew every year? Especially at your age, does this cycle of forgetting and searching hold any meaning?

These questions are too deep, too harsh, yet they are simple and real.

Another subtle tremor hit me. I always considered myself someone who loves plants, when in fact, for most plants, I have never gotten close to them, only observed from afar.

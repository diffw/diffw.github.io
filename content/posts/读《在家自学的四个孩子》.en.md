This book was written by a Christian mom from Taiwan. Unable to have children, she and her American husband adopted four children—two girls and two boys. The book chronicles their journey and practical experiences with homeschooling.

Intrigued by their family, I found this mom’s blog. It turns out they sold their apartment in Taipei in 2015 and moved to the clean, spacious countryside of Taitung, where they began building a farmhouse and selling agricultural products. They truly did what I dream of doing but lack the courage to attempt.

## Schools Are Not Necessarily the Best Place to Learn Social Skills

One major concern about homeschooling is that, without classmates, children might miss out on developing social skills. The author believes that schools and classmates are not necessarily the best way to learn social interaction for two reasons: First, there's limited peer-to-peer learning in terms of interpersonal skills; second, at school, interpersonal skills are mostly taught by teachers. However, given their limited time and energy, teachers often find it difficult to understand children deeply or reach their inner worlds.

In contrast, families, especially those with siblings under parental guidance, offer a solid foundation for learning social skills. I can relate to this deeply. My daughter and son often clash, but through those disagreements and parental guidance, they learn that "all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God," and slowly learn repentance, patience, forgiveness, and love.

## Not Reaching for the Top Is Not a Lack of Ambition but Wisdom

The author’s American husband was a tech expert in the U.S. and studied for another eight years in Taiwan. Many of his peers pursued high-powered jobs to showcase their talents, but their family chose freelancing from a family perspective, not aiming for the top.

For me, in my internet and design career, the pressure to climb higher is immense. I also know the burden and cost of reaching the top—often at the expense of health and family relationships. Being able to let go and not aim for the top requires wisdom and courage.

## What Kind of Person Do You Want to Raise?

Sometimes, the anxiety is overwhelming. Our generation grew up with teachings like "lagging behind means getting beaten" or "don't lose at the starting line" and "core competitiveness," and our nerves are taut when it comes to our children's education, fearing we might miss something.

When calm, you can feel the "human" aspect within us. Humans are not machines or parts of a societal system; we are soft, emotional, delicate, and thoughtful. We are also helpless and cannot foresee the future. What's in store for our children? What challenges will they face? Who can tell? But if asked what kind of people I want to raise my two children to become, I do have some basic answers.

The author states that humans are inherently valuable, not because of education, ability, or achievements, but because of the unique value given by God, the Creator. Absolutely.

## Goals for Different Stages

Reflecting on my educational journey, there are a few points for thought: Why study; interest in learning; and learning skills (methods). Back in my time, education was incredibly utilitarian—to escape from rural life and "earn a living from the state." Therefore, amid so many subjects and majors, very few allowed for experiencing the joy of learning, resulting in lost genuine interest. Politics, history, and geography should be fascinating topics but ended up dull.

The author defines learning goals for different stages. In elementary school, it's about learning life skills and habits (like household chores); in middle school, learning to think; and by high school, students should have mastered self-study skills, with parents only there to encourage. I wholeheartedly agree.

## Competitiveness and Quality

People often talk about "competitiveness." So, what exactly is competitiveness? Is it education, expertise, technical skills? Not entirely. In my nearly fifteen years of career experience, especially the past five or six years, I've interviewed many and worked alongside many. You can often tell in a few conversations or a few days of interaction whether someone is capable. If basic technical skills are there, the difference lies in quality, which includes upbringing, empathy, communication skills, and initiative.

The author says that, compared to competitiveness, it's more important to cultivate children's ability to interact, serve others, and learn independently. Education is not the filling of a pail, but the lighting of a fire. So true.

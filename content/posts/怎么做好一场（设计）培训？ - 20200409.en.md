Recently, I accepted an invitation from a friend to provide paid training for his company's design team.

I briefly reviewed my past design shares and considered the needs from a "empathy" angle for designers wanting to share. I outlined a syllabus for my friend—one topic per week, for a total of 8 sessions.

- Sharing Diff’s design career experiences & getting acquainted
- The dimensions of designers' abilities and professional ethics;
- Design communication: How should designers communicate requirements?
- How do designers critique their own (or others') designs?
- How do designers present their work?
- How to participate in interviews or interview others?
- How to manage a design team?
- How can designers improve themselves?

An important feedback from my friend was: We hope this is not just sharing, but something actionable, so employees can change. As he put it, "**We know too much, but can execute very little.**"

At this point, I realized that this "need" was different from most of the design shares I've done before. This is a training, not just a sharing session.

What is the biggest difference between sharing and training? Sharing is almost entirely input-oriented, listeners just "listen"; good training, however, requires output and implementation. Achieving awareness and behavioral change through action is the primary goal of training and is highly challenging.

I needed to consult someone on how to "implement" it properly. I reached out to my former colleague and friend Ning Jun, whose startup, **"Jian Du School"**, focuses on teaching how to put knowledge into practice. (Note: This is not a paid promotion)

After some discussions, Ning Jun recommended a book, **"Turning Training into Business Results: A Manager's Guide"**, and suggested I read it quickly. So I downloaded it on Kindle and spent two hours reading it after dinner. Here, I'd like to share my insights from this book about systematizing the concept of "training".

### **Training Must Address Business Needs**

Sometimes, when business owners or managers encounter an expert or industry figure, they naturally extend invitations like "Could you train our team?" But, in reality, the purpose at this point is often unclear. Training should be entirely tailored to business needs, extremely practical and operable; simultaneously, companies should implement mechanisms (frequent, timely performance feedback, incentive systems, etc.) to enable employees to identify their own shortcomings and feel incentivized to improve.

**Therefore, there are three optimal moments for training:**

- New employee onboarding: Quickly understanding the business;
- New project launch: Training on new skills and awareness needed for the project; (This wasn't mentioned in the book, just my summary)
- New job level training: Developing the comprehensive capabilities needed for higher positions;

### **First of All, Ask: What Are Your Business Goals?**

My friend is the company founder, and he initiated this training. Naturally, he is clear on the business goals. However, in slightly larger companies, the training is often initiated by the HR training department. Therefore, the training department's understanding of business goals becomes crucial.

Higher business goals and higher employee capabilities are mutually reinforcing. As a manager, if you vaguely feel that your employees lack capability, but can't pinpoint it, you must **re-evaluate your business goals**. Once the business goals are clear, you can discern if the performance and qualities of employees at every level align with those goals.

### **Can the Team/Employee Performance Support Business Goals?**

Through internal and external team surveys, you can evaluate from the "current strengths" and "current weaknesses" perspectives to determine if the team/employee performance supports the established business goals.

### **Four Major Factors of Poor Performance: Often Not About Employee Ability**

Employee performance is influenced by four major factors (4W):

- **World**: Uncontrollable external factors. For example, pandemics, regulations, competitors' actions, and more;
- **Workplace**: Internal company environment: organization structure, recruitment & compensation policies, company culture, management style;
- **Work**: The arrangement, process, tools, and system availability for specific tasks;
- **Worker**: Employee cognition, knowledge skills, work attitude, teamwork capabilities;

Often, when business stagnates and employee performance issues arise, companies oversimplify by claiming "our people are not up to par" or "so-and-so isn't capable." In fact, "people not being capable" is likely a minor factor. After all, why were they initially hired? 😄

### **If Performance Assessment Shows Employees Need Improvement, Then Consider Training**

Company founders and managers should treat training with the seriousness and attentiveness they apply to business and investments. Why is training important? Because the growth in employee cognition and capability can drive business and company growth. Having been involved in Liulishuo's early days, I understand this deeply.

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/04/133D6479-4691-4435-B8EE-9CEC5A8B5669-1024x817.jpg)

The cost and price of training don't just include expenses and HR resources. Repeatedly irrelevant or ineffective training not only wastes team/employee time but also gradually erodes their thirst for and trust in training, eventually affecting the company's learning and growth culture.

### **Conclusion: Training is a Systematic Project.** **To my understanding, there are three main components to training: Training Needs, Courses, Follow-Up & Assessment**

**1. Training Needs**

- Closely tied to business goals, using company culture, reward and punishment mechanisms (survival of the fittest), and frequent, timely performance feedback to motivate employees to improve their capabilities; 
- Successful training begins with thorough needs research (Reflecting on how I rashly accepted my friend's training request, quite fearless in my ignorance, a bit scary 😄);
- Management must acknowledge the importance of training and emphasize it at every opportunity;

**2. Courses**

- Course design, course expression, and course interaction by the trainer;

**3. Follow-Up & Assessment**

- Evaluate whether employees actively follow the training, change their attitudes, and apply training outcomes to their work, with corresponding reward and punishment mechanisms;
- Managers, especially basic managers, need to ensure training implementation: 1:1 communication, group sharing, etc.;
- Management and training departments should assess over a certain period the impact of past training on the business;

### **Common Reasons Why Training Fails**

- **Useless Training:** Lacks preliminary analysis of business goals and performance, attempts to solve "unsolvable" issues with training;
- **Wrong Training Audience:** The participants should be very targeted in terms of professional field and competency level; otherwise, it wastes some people's time (yet often, business owners think: since they're here, let's have more people listen in);
- **Inadequate Training Implementation:** Poor execution in training plans, course design, teaching. Another common mistake is flooding with knowledge in a short time;
- **Lack of Follow-Up:** This is the largest factor determining training success.

### **Finally, Attached is the Core Illustration from the Book - 6Ds**

![](http://diff.im/blog/wp-content/uploads/2020/04/A81D3788-F29D-406F-9AD2-9EBF9C42923F-1024x575.jpeg)

- **D1: Define the Business Outcomes:** —start with “why”; clarify the business rationale and expected results in business terms;
- **D2: Design the Complete Learning Experience:** —before, during, and after instruction;
- **D3: Deliver for Application:** —employ instructional strategies that facilitate application on the job;
- **D4: Drive Learning Transfer:** —provide structure, support, and accountability for using new skills and knowledge;
- **D5: Deploy Performance Support:** —provide learners with the job aids, coaching, apps, and other support they need to be successful;
- **D6: Document Results:** —measure, evaluate, and report the results to prove and continuously improve training’s value.

Overall, this is a highly practical book (offering principles, recommendations, and operational manuals). I recommend managers at all levels (and those preparing for management positions) give it a read.

For me, preparing this training course is a significant challenge, but I find it quite interesting. Perhaps, this is a direction for me.

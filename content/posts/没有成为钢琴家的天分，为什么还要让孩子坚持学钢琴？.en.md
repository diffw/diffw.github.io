Talent, in many cases, is a relative concept. One child might have a better sense of music, a steadier rhythm, better hands.

But when you've seen those truly gifted, you realize talent varies by orders of magnitude between people.

When it comes to my kids, I don't think they have the natural talent to become professional pianists. **But they have strong learning abilities, the capacity to receive lessons, and decent skills. For our family, that's enough.**

I've got three kids. My eldest daughter studied piano for about five or six years and stopped in 2024. My son started in 2023 and is still going. The youngest is two and a half, likely to start around four or five.

To talk about kids learning piano, I should start with my own art journey. To me, piano and drawing differ in form but both fall under the umbrella of learning the arts.

I started studying art in junior high, went through three years of professional training in high school, and continued with drawing and design in college. Lucky for me, I'm still designing.

But many of my classmates, whether from junior high or high school, are no longer in the field or doing any painting at all.

With this background in art education, I know learning piano is similar: those who stay and become pianists are rare—one in a hundred, one in a thousand.

If most kids aren't going to pursue a career in piano, why make them learn it?

Initially, like many others, we didn't know the real reasons. A friend's kid was learning piano, so we signed ours up too.

But with anything, **without clear goals, targets, and expectations from the start, it's easy to fall into a pit.** Art training especially: you start casually, then invest more and more—money, time, emotions, parent-child relationships—until eventually, it's hard to decide whether to continue or stop.

For our family, learning piano isn't about ensuring a professional path. It's about what piano can leave for them aside from that path. Here's what I think:

### A Way to Express Oneself

Not for performance, not just for exams. For me, piano's value is giving children another way to express themselves, to manage their emotions.

We all need different ways to express emotions. Drawing, writing, music, dance seem "useless." But often, these "useless" things provide an outlet. When words fail and emotions are unclear, they help express what's inside.

I casually played guitar for over ten years—and not well. But alone with a guitar, I can sing and play, letting my mind and body relax. Especially with hymns, I find peace for my soul.

My eldest is 16, started piano at 10, and kept at it for five or six years. At 15, she decided to stop. For us, it was tough. Did she dislike piano now? Was our investment wasted?

Turns out, it wasn't.

When overwhelmed by study or feeling low, she'd sit at the piano solo, playing her favorite pieces. Her immersion and relaxation during those moments showed me our time, money, and care weren't wasted.

**Piano became more than an "extracurricular class"; it became her way of being with herself.**

### Piano Isn't Just Finger Training

Piano isn't merely about finger skills; it's a whole-body activity.

People think of piano as a hand task—finger agility. But in actuality, it's far more. You must read scores, listen to sounds, control hand force and speed, use pedals, keep rhythm with your body, and process melody, rhythm, fingerings, and emotions in your head.

This isn't the job of a single organ but a unification of body, senses, attention, and understanding.

So when kids practice piano, they're not just learning "to play a piece." They're learning to make their bodies follow their attention, turning disparate parts into coordinated actions.

I find this crucial. **Learning might seem skill-focused, but deep down, it's training one's wholeness.**

### More Than Skills From a Good Teacher

A good teacher brings more than skills.

A good piano teacher doesn't just teach reading music, scales, and pieces. They teach in ways kids understand and reduce frustration by keeping their interest alive.

Like any art, skills are just part of piano. There’s music background, culture, and stories of composers and performers. These aspects make learning more than rote practice; it's entering an art world.

A good teacher shares life and learning experiences. Even if kids are young, these stories leave an impact. Kids not only gain knowledge but also form relationships.

Don't underestimate this bond. **Music is emotional expression, and expressing emotions isn’t just technical—it’s between people.**

If you view lessons as "I pay, you teach, class over," it might work, but it’ll be rigid.

### Learning Piano, Learning How to Learn

Piano teaches "how to learn a new skill"—a foundational, universal ability.

It shows in subjects and life. How to make fried rice, organize a room, swim, or handle new, tough tasks—same basics all around.

With the right teacher, kids learn a lot about learning itself: how to start, repeat, form habits—practicing actions to need no thought.

Also includes muscle memory.

At first, actions are clumsy—hand shape, fingerings, rhythm, reading sheet music, pedals—require practice so the body doesn’t need constant recalibration. But bad habits at the start need painful rewinding: identify the problem, slow down, dismantle, rebuild.

This hard task is invaluable. Because kids learn not just piano but learning's nature: **habits shape you, bad habits must be seen, and retraining is possible.**

These are more fundamental than "how many songs you can play."

### Building Resilience, Experiencing Growth

Happiness may be split: consumption vs. growth.

Consumption happiness is intense, quick. Eating, gaming, movies, social media—quick comfort.

Growth happiness is opposite. It starts dull, slow. Reading, painting, piano—practice leads to slight melody flow; many drawings yield tiny change.

Growth doesn’t addict because it demands boredom, delay, setbacks, and persistence to slowly savor "real progress."

Not saying consumption doesn’t matter; kids need ease, fun, rest. But as they grow, shape habits, develop taste, I prefer controlling too-easy pleasures, giving chances for slow-blooming joys.

### Some Practicality

I also hope kids, grown up, have a skill or hobby to connect with others. University or adulthood, singing and playing with friends—maybe form a casual band. Even without formal shows, just relaxing, music offering shared language, is nice.

So I don't think kids learning piano must be judged on "talent" or "making a living from it."

Of course, genuine talent and passion leading to a professional path is great. But for most, **arts don’t have to lead to careers to be worthwhile.**

They can offer new expression ways, self-space, coordination experience, and connections with teachers and music.

If one day they stop lessons but on a stressful night sit down to play a favorite song—**that makes it worth it.**

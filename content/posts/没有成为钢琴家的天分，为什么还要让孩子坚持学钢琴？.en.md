Talent is often relative. One child might have a better ear for music, steadier rhythm, or more agile fingers compared to another.

But when you meet those truly talented individuals, you'll see that talent can vary massively from person to person.

For my kids, I don't think they have the gift to become professional pianists. But they have strong learning abilities, they can absorb new skills, and they play decently. For us, that's enough.

I have three kids. Linda, the oldest, took piano lessons on and off for about five or six years and stopped in 2024. Anran started in 2023 and is still going. Eather, the youngest, is two and a half and might start around four or five.

When talking about kids and piano lessons, I should mention my own art journey. To me, piano and painting, though different in form, both fall under art education.

I started learning art in middle school, trained professionally through high school, and studied painting and design in college. I'm lucky to still work in design.

But many of my classmates, both in middle and high school, are no longer in this field or have stopped painting.

Having this background in art, I understand that for kids learning piano, staying in the field and becoming a pianist is rare, perhaps one in a hundred, or even one in a thousand.

If most kids won't pursue a professional piano path, why have them learn piano at all?

At first, like many others, I was pretty clueless. My friend's kids were learning, so why not sign ours up too?

However, without clear goals and expectations, things can go off track. Art training can especially snowball: starting "just to try," we end up investing more and more — money, time, emotions, and parent-child relationships all caught up until it's hard to say whether to continue or stop.

In our case, letting the kids learn piano isn't about betting on a professional future but thinking ahead to what the piano can offer beyond a career. Here are a few reasons I can think of.

### Developing a Way to Express Themselves

Not for performances or just for exams. To me, the piano's value is in giving kids another way to express themselves, to process emotions.

We all need outlets for our emotions. Drawing, writing, music, dance — these don't seem "useful." Yet these seemingly useless things provide a way out when words fail or emotions overwhelm.

I've mindlessly strummed a guitar for over a decade. I'm terrible. But alone, I sing along, relaxing my body and mind, especially when praising God in song, feeling my soul come to rest.

Linda, my daughter, persisted with piano from age 10, stopping around 16. Making that choice was tough. We wondered: does she no longer like piano? Was it all a waste?

Over time, I saw it's not the case.

When she's stressed or low, she sits at the piano and plays favorites. She's focused and relaxed.

It shows our time, money, and support weren't wasted.

**Piano's become more than just an "extracurricular." It's how she spends time with herself.**

### Piano Isn't Just Finger Training

Playing piano isn't just about finger skills. It's a full-body activity.

Many think piano is just about hand movements. But truly playing involves much more. Eyes read the music, ears listen, hands control pressure and speed, feet work pedals, the body maintains rhythm, and the mind processes melody, rhythm, technique, and emotion.

It's not just a single organ at work but a unification of body, senses, focus, and intellect.

So piano practice isn't just about "can you play a piece." Kids learn to have their bodies obey their focus and integrate many parts into one smooth action.

I find this key. **Learning often appears about mastering a skill, but deeper down it's about training one's wholeness.**

### More Than Skills from a Good Teacher

With the right teacher, kids learn beyond skills.

A good piano teacher isn't just about reading music, playing scales, or getting through songs. More importantly, they teach in a way that kids grasp, protecting their interest, and offering encouragement when needed.

Like other arts, skills are part of piano. But there's the musical background, cultural knowledge, stories of composers and performers. These make learning more than repeated practice, slowly opening the world of art.

A great teacher shares their life and learning too. Even if just a child listens, these stories leave a mark. Kids gain knowledge and form a bond with the teacher.

Don't overlook this relationship. Music itself is emotional expression, and that's always human to human.

If piano lessons are just a "paid service," they can work but might be stiff learning.

### Learning Piano Teaches How to Learn

Learning piano teaches "how to learn a new skill," a foundational and broad ability.

It's visible in academics and life. How to learn fried rice, organize a room, swim, or handle something you're initially bad at, all traces back to similar lessons.

With a good teacher, kids learn much about learning piano: how to start, repeat, form habits, practice till actions become automatic.

This includes muscle memory.

Many actions start clumsy. Hand positions, techniques, rhythm, reading, pedaling all take practice until the body stops recalculating each time. If poor habits form early, unlearning them is painful: first identify the problem, slow down, dismantle old actions, and rebuild.

It's hard but valuable. Kids learn more than piano but learning's very nature: habits shape people, bad ones can be seen, and can be retrained.

These lessons go deeper than "playing a few more pieces."

### Building Resilience and Experiencing Growth Joy

Pleasure may split into consuming versus growth.

Consuming pleasures are intense and quick: a great meal, a game, a movie, short videos or social media, delivering fast comfort.

Growth pleasures are the opposite: initially mild stimuli with slow feedback. Reading, painting, piano are like this: you practice long and maybe get a part slightly smoother; create many drawings to see even a small change.

Growth pleasures don't easily addict or immediately satisfy kids. They need patience with boredom, slowness, failures, and repeated efforts to savor "I really improved" joys.

I don't say consuming pleasures are unneeded. Kids need leisure, relaxation, and rest. Yet when still forming habits, tastes, and aesthetics, I'm careful with easy pleasures, allowing more chances to taste joys that need time and resilience.

### A Bit of Practicality

I hope my kids grow up with a skill or hobby connecting them to others in certain situations. Like in college or adulthood, singing or playing with friends, even forming a small, impromptu band. Not for performance, but just an easy gathering, adding a shared language through music.

So, I don't think judging kids' piano by "talent" or "can they make a living from it" is necessary.

If a child truly has talent and passion, aiming professionally, wonderful. For most, though, **art doesn't need to lead to a career to be worthwhile.**

It can simply give kids another way to express themselves, a space to be with themselves, coordination experience, and a means of connecting with teachers and music.

If someday, a child no longer takes piano lessons but in a stressful night, still opts to sit and play a favorite tune,

I think that's worth it.

Since I was young, I've loved fishing. Like many of my other hobbies, I'm not particularly skilled and tend to procrastinate, but my interest has never waned over time.

I've always pursued simplicity, practicality, and functionality (though it doesn't necessarily mean cheap). I try to buy as little as possible and use what I have as much as possible. In 2021, I noticed some friends getting into lure fishing, which piqued my interest in this style of fishing. During the winter, amidst the pandemic and online classes, we returned to our home in Changsha. In my spare time, I started experimenting with lure fishing.

Looking back, buying the lure fishing set (a domestic Guangwei Small Yellow Reel) was probably something I regret. Despite all the strong warnings in articles and videos against "buying a set," I eventually decided to "just start playing." To my novice friends thinking about trying lure fishing, I would also advise against buying a set. Instead, look at the fish species in your area and get a worthy set right from the start, even if it pinches your wallet a bit. Clearly, my advice will likely be ignored just as easily. 😄

The Small Yellow Reel set tangled

As with any other interest or skill, getting into lure fishing requires spending time, effort, and money. This involves selecting the right equipment, loading and tying leaders/hooks, using different fishing rigs (soft/hard bait) and their basic techniques, casting (overhead/side cast), preventing and untangling line tangles, etc. Thanks to having a fish pond at our home in Changsha, and a paid lure fishing base 6 kilometers away, I stopped being a beginner after paying some learning costs.

Once I reached the point where I could consistently catch fish at several lure ponds and rarely tangled the line, I upgraded my equipment. I spent 2,600 RMB on a fishing rod and reel (SHIMANO's Glorious II M rod, DAIWA's ALPHAS Japanese model reel). It seems any modern activity, when it comes to equipment, is a bottomless pit (e.g., photography, cycling). So, make sure it's affordable and good enough. Good equipment offers better performance and experience. At the very least, for a basic lure fishing enthusiast like me, it gives peace of mind as you won't have to worry about some errors being caused by poor equipment, like line tangling due to equipment performance or insufficient casting distance. Also, admittedly, this set boosts my confidence since it doesn’t reveal I'm a beginner with "Small Yellow" gear—even though equipment alone doesn’t guarantee successful fishing.

I spent quite a bit on lure fishing at black pits (paid lure ponds). In Changsha, it costs 240 RMB / 180 RMB for initial fishing after restocking, and 100-140 RMB for fishing at other times, typically once a week. But thinking about it, we only lived in Changsha for two months, so it wasn't that frequent or costly. It's hard for my parents to understand spending so much on fishing. I understand them, but I have my own thoughts and way of living. Beyond basic needs, discovering and nurturing a hobby, if possible, is a luxury and worthwhile. The joy of living lies in this; otherwise, life would be really monotonous.

Traditional pole fishing involves setting a spot, casting a line, and sitting while watching the float for fish. In contrast, lure fishing is "active." It involves standing, moving around to find fish, repeatedly casting, manipulating the rod/line, and directly feeling fish bites through the rod. I find the process of pole fishing, where after hooking a fish you lift and retrieve the rod to feel the strength of the fish, to be the most exciting, but the thrill from lure fishing is even greater. The added excitement comes from the clear sensation in your hands when a fish bites, as if knocking twice. For me, the peak of enjoyment and excitement is feeling the fish bite, rather than the quantity or capturing them. As long as there's a photo or video for social media bragging or memorabilia, that's enough. Conversely, for me, the excitement pinnacle with pole fishing is the final tally of fish caught and taking them home. This difference might be why lure fishing advocates catch-and-release practices.

I jokingly told a friend it would be too cruel to "abandon wife and kids" for fishing alone on weekends, so I turned lure fishing into a family activity. I first taught my twelve-year-old daughter some basic lure skills, like casting, basic techniques, and fish-handling tips. She quickly found joy in it. Next, I taught my seven-year-old son. Last week, at a lure fishing base near Shanghai, while guiding my son, I taught my wife some basic casting (setting the brake to maximum) and techniques. After catching ten fish, she seems hooked too.

By the way, in English, 'lure fishing' means fishing with bait, representing temptation. Simply put, it involves using realistic soft or hard baits and suitable techniques to mimic small water creatures' movements, thus luring target fish to bite. As my daughter says, it's "tricking fish." Humans can be so cheeky. But, sometimes I really admire the person who invented lure fishing—it was truly a genius idea.

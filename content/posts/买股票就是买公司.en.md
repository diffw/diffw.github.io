Continuing with "Value Investing and China" by Li Lu.

**One of the core principles of value investing is that buying stocks means buying a company**. This is a philosophy always upheld by Warren Buffett and was introduced by his teacher, Benjamin Graham.

When I first encountered this concept, I was able to understand and appreciate it, as I've had some personal experiences. In 2013, when I chose to [join the newly established team at Liulishuo on half salary](http://mp.weixin.qq.com/s?__biz=MzAxNjgyODc4OA==&mid=2651665564&idx=1&sn=39df4cb7392b015fe49aee5fe439c8c7&chksm=80174487b760cd91a9c29175b2f90f8b050d521af3007f83e2b9eca4a3bc9df2b7e4e9bc5321&scene=21#wechat_redirect), I did so with the mindset of investing in Liulishuo. Also, while working at Liulishuo, most of the time (to be honest, there were moments of distraction), I approached people and matters with the mindset of a "company owner."

Let me give two very specific examples: Once, I saw a security guard playing on his phone and I went over to hold him accountable, "What are you doing during work hours?" When dining in the cafeteria, I noticed a colleague casually throwing the serving spoon into the rice pot after serving, and I directly pointed out, "Hey, that's not right." **These two things were completely outside my job scope, but I "expanded" to the point of considering myself the company owner, to put it simply, acting like the boss.** Particularly with the security guard example, I suppose only a boss (and a stingy one at that) would care about such details, right?

The "investment" in Liulishuo back then resulted in quite a good return. This positive feedback and experience helped me understand the "buying stocks means buying a company" concept very well.

In practicing this principle, Li Lu’s approach is very interesting. He believes that since I bought a company's stock with an "investor" mindset (rather than a speculative mindset), I am indeed an investor and owner of that company. **Even if you've only bought 1 share, don't be embarrassed; in reality, you own the proportionate ownership of that 1 share.** As an investor, you certainly have the right, and it is very necessary, to understand the operations of the company you've invested in.

Therefore, Li Lu likes to conduct on-site "inspections" of the companies he's invested in. Of course, not all companies allow him the opportunity for an inspection. When he's turned away, he might even chat amiably with the security guards outside, discussing their work conditions and feelings. In this way, he can indirectly understand the company’s operations, corporate culture, etc. Truly the efforts of a smart investor.

Soon, I started imitating the master. Recently, I've taken on the "owner's" mindset and have been visiting the stores of several companies I’ve invested in, one by one, such as NIO, Xiaomi, Apple, and Starbucks. (This is not an investment recommendation 😄)

In reality, the shops are the same as before, but my attitude, perspective, and approach to visiting have changed. Now, I’m inspecting as a boss – disguised, with a very "expanded" mindset. Here I check the foot traffic and listen to what guests are saying and how they evaluate our products; there I observe the quality of service and morale of our employees. Occasionally, I even pose as a potential customer and chat with the staff.

"Incognito inspection" doesn’t have much significance with a single visit; it must be done consistently over time. By persistently understanding a company's products and services, one should be able to feel the differences and trends at various stages. These insights can enhance understanding of a company and inform value judgments.

I shared a rough idea with a friend: If you're doing value investing full time, first, when choosing to invest in a company, you should see yourself as joining that company (viewing yourself as its owner or employee works), engaging with products and people realistically, experiencing service sincerely, and walking alongside the company for several years. It’s much like joining a startup team as a key member. Secondly, you should only choose to invest in a limited number of companies. People’s interests, knowledge, abilities, and energy are limited.

The concept of "buying stocks means buying a company" applies not just in the realm of value investing; I believe it's also relevant in the workplace.  When you’re deciding whether to join a company, you need to make these judgments:

- Does the company’s product and service have real customer value and a broad market growth potential?
- Do the company’s products and services match your career interests and professional abilities?
- Can this company provide you enough space in the coming years to grow, take on responsibilities, and create your own hallmark works?
- Do you recognize and respect the founders, management team, and direct leaders’ character and abilities?
- Do you understand and agree with the company’s values and culture?

Frequent job changes, especially those made for better pay or to escape challenges, are almost like speculators in the stock market. Be smart about considering the long-term.

Additionally, I've decided to regularly spend some time each week getting out and exploring. With an "incognito inspection" mindset, I will engage with different products and industries, sometimes to experience and consume, and also to communicate with different people. I believe that this will, over time, help me understand the value of businesses and the companies I invest in.

Staying closed at home, or in the company, or in a fixed circle, is bound to fail.

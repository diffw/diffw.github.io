A few years ago, I was dining with my family at a buffet restaurant in Sanya. The restaurant wasn't upscale, but they had hired a girl to play the piano in the center. Despite the piano melodies filling the air, the few diners present were more focused on their meals than the music.

Seeing the scene, I felt a sense of pity and regret. I couldn't help but wonder, how much does the girl earn from playing? Does she enjoy the music while playing, or is it merely a means of making a living? Delving deeper, I questioned the value of art: is music just background to enhance dining, and are paintings merely to decorate restroom walls?

Returning to the topic of children learning to paint.

I started learning sketching and coloring in middle school, and for three years of high school and the first two years of university, I underwent foundational training in sketching and color. After that, I've been engaged in design work related to art. Thus, I have some professional background to discuss this topic.

A child's time is precious, and any extra training time cuts into the already limited time they have for play, entertainment, and rest amid heavy academic demands. Childhood growth requires adequate free time and space. Before enrolling a child in an art class, we need to reflect on one question: **Why do we want the child to learn painting?**

### **Starting with the most practical aspect: what are the career prospects of painting?**

In my high school and university combined, I had about 120 classmates. Of them, less than 20 are still working in art-related fields (artists, designers, art teachers, etc.). Only about 2-3 make a living directly through painting. From a worldly perspective, these artist classmates don't have the best economic conditions.

From my observations and discussions with friends and colleagues, this isn't an isolated case. The reality is that most fine arts graduates don't necessarily work in art-related jobs (which isn't necessarily a bad thing), and the salary level for most art-related workers is average or even below average. My point is that considering a career path in art for children has many uncertainties. **Not many can thrive or even make a living solely from art.**

Since so few people can make a living from painting, what is the use of painting? Forgive me for using such a blunt and crude phrase like "what's the use." But if we speak purely pragmatically, painting isn't of much use. However, if parents can adjust their mindset and update their perspectives on painting, then painting does have some uses. Let's discuss three aspects briefly.

### **Painting is a way for children to express themselves**

Have you noticed that almost all children start painting before they can speak? They don't just paint on paper; they're also enthusiastic about drawing on any materials their young bodies can reach: walls, tables, clothes, their bodies, and so on.

Every child is naturally an artist. Language needs to be taught to children, but painting is almost innate. Children learn it without a teacher and are incredibly confident. When you place a pen and paper in their hands, no child says, "I can't paint." They take the pen and paper, and without thinking, confidently and boldly draw and scribble lines and images.

From 1-2-year-old toddlers aimlessly scribbling on paper, to 4-5-year-olds drawing more recognizable pictures, to 7-8-year-olds and beyond who can tell stories through their drawings—each stage, whether abstract or concrete, is a way for children to express through art.

During early childhood, children use drawing to express their understanding of the world created by God: plants, animals, everyday items, tools, vehicles, and people. As they grow older, children use drawing to release the whimsy of their imagination and thoughts. Sometimes, children who think more deeply might express their emotions, likes, and dislikes through painting.

Unfortunately, many parents tend to overlook this form of expression, assuming children are just scribbling. However, if you pay close attention, you'll find the joy children express through painting.

### **Painting cultivates aesthetics (appreciation of beauty) and imagination**

Aesthetics is a very abstract and subjective matter. Cultivating aesthetics is a long and slow process.

For some, aesthetics might seem unimportant, "as if it can't be a living." But having good aesthetics, externally, facilitates interactions with others who appreciate and accept you. Beyond the external, aesthetics enriches a person's interior life. When you develop a sense of aesthetics, whether in dress or choice of objects, or in arts like music and visual arts, you can enjoy the pleasure of "appreciation, taste, and experience."

In enhancing and improving aesthetics, painting is an all-encompassing creative activity. Painting involves using the mind, sparking imagination, observing with the eyes, presenting with the hands, expressing with the mouth, and listening with the ears.

Among them, I want to highlight a crucial aspect of the painting process: observation. Through their eyes, children observe the world created by God—trees, the sky, the earth, the ocean, animals, and people. In observing, children experience and appreciate the beauty of shapes and colors and the relationships between things: size, contrast, warmth, and so on.

Besides enhancing aesthetics, painting can also shape a person's imagination. Painting is a creative activity, and every piece of artwork is a unique creation. Through painting, children can unleash and activate the rich and limitless imagination in their minds. Imagination is nearly synonymous with creativity. Without creativity, life is so dull.

### **Painting fosters visual thinking**

While not everyone will become artists or make a living from painting, in life, we often need some painting skills, such as sketching to communicate and express ideas.

In some communications, language might be refined, yet participants still find it abstract, leading to different understandings of the same words. At such times, visual thinking and hands-on skills let you quickly transform language into tangible images through drawing or sketches.

For example, you might use a flowchart to visually explain a complex work arrangement. You can also communicate simple ideas for home décor to renovation companies through a floor plan sketch. You might even use color to convey style preferences and ideas. 
In the end, drawing, like language, music, and dance, is a way to express oneself, build relationships with others, and communicate with the outside world. Additionally, painting is a highly personal activity, letting one have an independent world to observe, feel, and express.

Lastly, let's discuss a few concerns parents often have:

### **Does my child have potential in painting?**

First, this question is rather odd.

I've noticed that if a child's parents have an art background, everyone (including the parents) will confidently praise the child's painting abilities, saying "This child truly has artistic talent."

But with other children, even if they show a love and aptitude for painting, their parents, working in business or STEM fields, are the first to question, "Does my child truly have potential in painting?"

This doubt seems to imply that a child's artistic ability is inherited from the parents.

But that's not the case. As mentioned earlier, every child in their early years is a very confident artist. No child says they can't paint; they just freely and instinctively put their imaginations on paper.

You don't need to question your child's potential in drawing; every child is naturally an artist.

### **How to appreciate a child's artwork?**

This is a challenge almost every parent faces.

Whether parents want to actively praise and encourage their child's passion for painting or are responding to their child's queries like, "Mom, Dad, how is my drawing?" most parents find it difficult to appreciate and evaluate their child's art.

Particularly for pictures drawn by children under six, which are often abstract and disorderly, parents struggle to find an entry point for appreciation and can't determine what's depicted, making evaluation challenging. Reluctantly, they offer sincere but generic compliments: "You did an amazing job, keep it up, great work."

Recently, I read a book on art education, "Nurturing Children from Drawing" by Akira Torii, a renowned Japanese educator and artist. He suggests appreciating a child's art by "listening" to it.

How do you listen to a drawing?

It's simple: ask questions about the artwork and listen to the child's explanations. For example, asking, "Can you tell me what this part is? Why did you paint it this way?" Of course, offer some encouraging words now and then, but mainly listen to the child discuss their art.

Like any art, creation isn't complete until appreciated, and as Akira Torii emphasizes, **for children, their artistic creation isn't complete until their creative ideas are spoken.** Therefore, he encourages parents to interact with their child's artwork through "listening."

I recently discovered this approach while reading the book, but coincidentally, during my children's upbringing, I inadvertently used "listening" to appreciate their paintings. After completing a piece, I would either invite my children to tell the whole story behind their artwork or engage in a Q&A format. I'd often document these interactions with videos, and looking back, they were both fun and valuable.

### **Should I send them to art classes?**

Firstly, when your child shows interest in painting, I don't believe there's a need to immediately enroll them in art classes. Notably, many art classes on the market follow a standardized, assembly-line teaching approach. Teachers sometimes even assist in drawing to enhance the final work, pleasing parents.

As for when to send them? I can't provide a precise answer. For my own children, I believe in not suppressing their enthusiasm for painting and allowing for natural, free development. Someday, if my child says, "Dad, I want to learn art," I consider this a signal for formal instruction.

### **How to create an artistic environment for children?**

First, provide basic art supplies. Most households have pencils, crayons, and watercolor pens, but often overlook "paper," with many children drawing in sketchbooks or on single sheets of blank paper.

Here, **I strongly recommend parents buy dedicated sketchbooks or drawing pads.** They're affordable, requiring an annual investment of about 100-200 yuan. Using a dedicated drawing book adds a sense of ceremony (the child feels recognized and supported) and effectively preserves their works, forming a thick collection over time (as opposed to loose sheets, which are difficult for parents to organize).

A friendly reminder to parents: don't begrudge the use of paper (or money), and don't overly focus on what the child has drawn. Sometimes, a child might scribble impulsively or "doodle away" a new sheet of paper. As a parent, this might feel "painful" to witness.

Second, create opportunities for children to think through drawings and imagery. For instance: What birthday gift do you wish for? Draw it. How would you like this year’s Christmas to be? Sketch it...

Third, listen to your child discuss their artwork. As mentioned, appreciating their art involves "asking questions and listening," such as "What story does this drawing tell?" or "This looks interesting, can you tell Mom or Dad what it's about?"

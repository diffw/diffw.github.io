On March 29th of this year, I left an organization where I had worked for 5 years to become an independent developer.

Five years is not a short time. It was a significant period of transition for me because I immigrated from China to the United States during this time.

I am grateful to my colleagues; it was a great team with a conducive environment for work and growth. Even so, I felt it was time to move to the next stage or make a change.

At the end of 2025, I started using AI tools like Cursor and Claude for design work and even created some tangible products. This felt wonderful.

It felt almost like 2005 again, when I was obsessed with making Flash websites, personal sites, and creative designs (often called "airplane drafts"). But now, with AI, even designers without any coding knowledge can create products that users can actually use.

I set aside all other hobbies such as fishing and woodworking to spend all my time on AI-driven projects.

But, there is never enough time. To realize these ideas, I've been sacrificing my rest periods and time with family, wife, and children. Approaching 40, I don’t have the energy I once did. Over time, the lack of energy and physical stamina leads to aches and blurred vision.

Slowly, I felt the need for more complete time to realize my ideas. These ideas aim firstly to solve problems I, my family, and friends encounter in life. I believe they could also help more people like us and bring about corresponding rewards.

Thus, the idea of leaving my organization began to grow in my mind.

I went through a lot of inner conflict and thought, and had many discussions with my wife.

First, there’s the relatively stable source of income. After leaving, this becomes unstable. Especially for someone who grew up fearing poverty, overcoming this requires some mental breakthroughs.

Then there's the team. Collaborations inevitably involve friction and energy drain, but being part of a team provides a supportive atmosphere, interpersonal exchanges, care, and interaction.

In contrast, being an independent developer can feel lonelier and requires more effort in learning and growth. There is no natural environment for learning and sharing outside of the team.

There's also the sense of meaning. The work my previous team did was valuable and meaningful. Frankly, choosing to become an independent developer is not a decision I’m 100% sure about; it feels more like exploration, potentially leading to a loss of social identity. In social gatherings, people introduce others with titles, garnering recognition. As an independent developer, one becomes more obscure.

I did a few things to clarify my thoughts. Throughout, I continuously communicated with my wife, hoping she would understand and support me.

First, I reviewed our family's financial situation, using AI to help with some financial analysis.

I estimated data from two aspects:
1. Assets: including current savings and stock investments
2. Expenses: including family living costs in the US, short and long-term education and medical costs for our three children, and various taxes

By comparing these, I was roughly sure that in the coming years, I have the conditions to explore a different path for a while.

I started documenting my thoughts and feelings daily, gradually extracting the ideas in my head into words. The core idea is, I want to give myself a chance to try, at least once in this lifetime.

From 2005 to 2026, I have worked for 21 years. Despite being seen by friends and others as adventurous for working at startups, I am actually very cautious, timid, and risk-averse. I have always joined others' startups, never giving myself a chance to realize my own ideas.

I am 43 now, nearing 50. Perhaps now is the prime time to seize the opportunity to try. Even if I fail, I won’t regret it. But why must failure be the outcome? Why can’t I succeed?

These thoughts and dilemmas lasted a while. Until one day, I reached a balanced point and felt peaceful inside.

At that moment, I could choose to continue working at the organization, or leave and start my exploration. At that point, the two choices were balanced.

So, I decided to break this balance: I chose to leave.

My colleagues asked why it was so sudden. But for me, it wasn't sudden; I had been thinking and debating it for some time, though the decision-making was swift. That’s my style.

After announcing my departure to the team, I shared these thoughts with my two elder children.

My eldest daughter supports me in giving myself a chance to realize my ideas and admires my decision. My 12-year-old son, on the other hand, is concerned about whether our finances are secure and worries about money.

I reassured him, "As long as you have breakfast to eat every morning, our financial situation is not a problem."

Now, as an independent developer for three weeks, I've launched a small macOS tool (with several iterations) and a Bible Widget iOS app (purely for practice to learn more about iOS features, not expecting to update it further).

I can't say the process is very interesting, but it’s certainly a unique experience.

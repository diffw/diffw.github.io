A year ago, when I saw the introduction to this book, I judged it to be a book that would inevitably be strongly criticized domestically, so I promptly ordered, purchased, and collected it.

Surprisingly, this book is still around. My unreliable guess is that there are two reasons. First, its distribution has been too limited to attract enough attention. Second, the author's expression is very restrained, knowing where the bottom line is. For example, the opening line: from the Qin and Han to the Ming and Qing, it was the "Qin System Era" in China, or the "Qin Government Era." In fact, after reading the entire book, regarding the governance strategies, policies, and methods of the Qin system, I believe we, who live in the present, will inevitably have some empathic understanding. Therefore, the author is restrained.

I'm actually not much of a reader. Reading is quite a "heavy" thing for me; I can't enjoy it as easily as snacking. I rarely touch history books. On one hand, the rote teaching style in history classes during my school days, where teachers read from textbooks and students had to memorize dates, figures, places, and events, completely ruined my appetite. On the other hand, history books available through normal channels are all written by victors, making truth hard to discern. I picked up "The Qin System Over Two Thousand Years" on the recommendation of a trusted friend. A third of the way through, I couldn't help but exclaim: how great would it be if school history teachers were of the author's caliber! Discussing history certainly involves elements like time, figures, places, and events. But individual elements, or even their combinations, are dry and devoid of value and meaning. I think the most important aspect of reading history is to discern the thread of historical development, cause-and-effect relationships, and the warnings and lessons for individuals, organizations, and countries of that era. In this book, the author catches the main thread of the rise and fall of the "Qin System," leading readers through one dynasty era after another, showing the ruthlessness of the decisions made by the rulers as well as the sufferings of the common people.

The essence of the Qin system boils down to two points: extraction and control of the common people. From the Qin dynasty to the Ming and Qing (let's say restrained), the rulers mulled over how to establish a more stable and efficient system of extraction to obtain more human and material resources from the populace. Related to this, there were methods to control the people's thoughts, knowledge, and speech. These methods included keeping the populace occupied with their livelihoods so they had no time for anything else, implementing policies of ignorance, controlling speech, and instituting neighbor-reporting systems. From the Qin to the Ming and Qing, rulers of various dynasties almost employed the same tactics. Though dynasties changed, the system essentially remained the same. How can one not heave a deep sigh, realizing that from the Qin to the Ming and Qing, over two thousand years passed and China was unable to break this cycle of history, only to run in place? The people's suffering was immense. I think the willingness to be enslaved in people's hearts does not stop abruptly at any dynasty. It is hard to deny that such a willingness to be enslaved isn't flowing in our veins today. The road ahead is long.

The book also reframed many of my understandings of historical events. For instance, the various "Golden Ages" and "Enlightened Rules" once lauded in history classes are actually contrary to the textbook descriptions. Du Fu's poem "Recollecting the Glorious Days of Yore" (In the thriving days of the Kaiyuan reign, even small towns sheltered thousands of households. Rice was like oil, corn was white, public and private granaries were abundantly stocked.) was the core material of posterity's praise of Emperor Xuanzong's "Kaiyuan Prosperity," yet Du Fu wrote this while living in turmoil during the An Lushan Rebellion. Beautiful verses were merely the poet's post-facto glorification of peaceful days past. In contrast, lines also written by Du Fu, like "Beyond the vermilion gates, wine and meat reek; on the road lie bones of the frozen—such is reality during Emperor Xuanzong’s time."

Behind these heavy topics, there are also some interesting stories. For example, Liu Bang originated from the "wandering knights" group, Tang Taizong Li Shimin spent his life performing the act of "accepting advice as if flowing," and Yongzheng's secret memorials led to his thirteen years of emperorship being thirteen years devoted to sorting through rubbish memorials, with an average of more than eight thousand words responded to daily. These narratives are both amusing and exasperating.

Although this book is still available on the market, I am still pessimistic about its longevity. Treasure it while you can, and quickly place your order and collect it.

I noticed that many people in the community have read this book and shared some notes about it. So I took some time to skim through it myself.

Especially as I'm doing indie development, books like "Building the Right Product" easily catch my interest. Who doesn't want to build the right product?

Sadly, I feel this book doesn't have much to offer or much substantial content. It's a book you can skim-read quickly. In the AI era, the ideas and methods in the book just don't match today's real-world situation or needs. This isn't just about this book—many books about processes and methods, like current software, may just be discarded. Historical or theoretical studies tend to have more staying power, fighting against time's flow.

However, there are a few points I found valuable.

First, it kept reminding me of a basic concept, almost like drilling it into me: most products fail most of the time. This "cold splash" of reality is harsh, but reality is reality. This splash isn't completely negative. It prepared me, showing that succeeding in creating something isn't easy; failure is the norm. What I need to do is try again and again.

"Try again and again" is something Zhang Xue frequently says. I like Zhang Xue and his heartfelt words. For instance: "If you don't do it when you're young, you'll regret it when you're old. Even if you fail, you won't feel regret." (This even drove me to start indie development.) And: "If I don't have others' talent or resources but work ten times harder, why shouldn't the result be mine? It should be mine."

In short, making products, failure is normal. You need persistence.

Another striking idea was the Pretotype concept he mentioned. We usually know the prototype, but the author introduced "Pretotyping" instead.

What's Pretotyping?

The book uses the Chinese term "预型," which comes before a prototype. In industrial design, a "prototype" is a basic, working version; but the book's "Pretotype" is something with form and feel, without any real functionality.

It mentioned the founder of Palm phones. After a costly development failure, he thought of Pretotyping. He wanted to create a handheld phone, but the technology wasn't mature. So he made a phone shape from a block of wood, printed some screens that looked realistic, and stuck them on. He even used a chopstick as a stylus.

To understand real user needs and experiences, he carried this "Pretotype" to meetings and with clients. Whenever he needed to order, note things, or plan tasks, he'd take out this wooden phone and the chopstick stylus, doing pretend operations.

He did this for a long time. Although it looked strange, it tremendously helped in developing the final product. This example stuck with me.

The final valuable reminder: after your MVP, you need to release it, reach real users and gather data. This was enlightening.

In the past, I hesitated to take this step, not wanting to spend early promotion money, thinking posting in social circles might be enough. But honestly, for real user feedback early on, necessary promotion is essential.

Other parts didn't leave much of an impression. Overall, it's a book for quick reading.

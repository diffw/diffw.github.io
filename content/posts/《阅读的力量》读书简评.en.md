Fortunately, both my children—a 12-year-old daughter and a 7-year-old son—love and enjoy reading. Sometimes, they get so engrossed that we have to limit their reading time to protect their eyesight.

Friends occasionally ask me how to make their kids enjoy reading since their children prefer video games or short videos. The reason it's only occasionally asked is that most of my friends are Christians, and nearly all Christian children enjoy reading.

After reading "The Power of Reading" recommended by Mr. Guo Chuyang, I gained a comprehensive understanding of what we did right about "children's reading" at home.

In "The Power of Reading," "reading" refers to Free Voluntary Reading (FVR), where readers read purely for enjoyment, without needing to write book reports or answer questions at the end of chapters. If they don't like the current book, they don't have to finish it and can switch to another that interests them.

Just read the books you're interested in? Comics, novels, magazines—all are acceptable? According to the author, yes, they are. This viewpoint challenged my beliefs. I always thought comics, novels, magazines were at best "leisure reading," suitable for relaxation and entertainment, but don't expect to improve reading skills, language proficiency, or cultural literacy through them. The author supports his view by citing various direct and indirect experiments, surveys, and studies. He argues that these "light reads" can spark interest in reading and develop reading skills, eventually leading readers to higher-level and more challenging texts.

Reflecting on my son's reading journey, I agree with this viewpoint. My 7-year-old son transitioned from picture books to pure text largely influenced by his sister, and his "light read" was the "Harry Potter" series, which his sister frequently read and discussed. Stimulating interest in reading through an engaging book is the core tenet of FVR.

A good reading environment is also crucial. The author suggests "creating opportunities to be close to books." We have a well-stocked home library, clean and orderly bookshelves, plenty of light, and comfortable chairs and sofas—all key elements. Additionally, we do two things: one, we don't have a TV at home, and two, we strictly control the use of iPhones/iPads. When my daughter was in first grade, her principal Ms. C advised parents to keep children away from video games and highly stimulating videos to maintain a healthy, moderate taste. While we don't watch TV, we have a family movie night every Sunday.

Aside from the environment, the influence of "peers" is very significant, such as classmates and friends. Sometimes kids read certain books due to "peer pressure" or "peer interest trends." Beyond their peers, parental influence is immense. In households where parents don't read and instead play games or scroll on phones all day, it's hard to imagine children developing a love for reading.

Should you offer special rewards for a child's "reading"? No! Because the joy that reading brings is the greatest reward itself. I completely agree. When people read for a reward, reading just becomes a means to an end. The focus on personal gain can entirely kill interest.

The author also discusses the relationship between reading and writing. A surprising perspective is that a person's writing style is not developed through writing experience but through reading. Reflecting on my writing style (if I have one), I believe it’s heavily influenced by the Chinese Union Version of the Bible—straightforward, using short sentences, with virtually no elaborate or obscure words.

Reading has power. Perhaps even those who don’t love reading believe this. I think that in "The Power of Reading," author Stephen Krashen, by citing over 300 references (surveys, academic papers, etc.) to support his claims, is ultimately telling us that free and interest-driven reading will empower us and our children with the strength of reading.

### **The Cause**

Around 5 PM last Wednesday, my wife picked up the children from school and brought them downstairs. She called me to say they were home. I told her I’d come down to exercise with Linda for half an hour, and then go back home to do homework.

Why did I think Linda should use some time to exercise? Her studies are quite intense, and her body needs adjustment and rest. The night before, she struggled with a math problem and didn’t sleep until after 10 PM. I set aside the work I was busy with, grabbed Linda’s jump rope, and enthusiastically headed downstairs.

When I saw Linda, I barely finished saying, "Linda, let’s go jump rope for half an hour," when her previously smiling face immediately turned into a look of "bitterness and hatred."

She quietly responded, "I have a lot of homework today. If I don’t finish it, it’s your fault." This comment started to make me feel a little uncomfortable.

"Come on, you need to exercise for a bit, just half an hour. That way, you can keep healthy," I insisted that we should go exercise together. I started walking toward the neighborhood exercise area. Linda reluctantly followed far behind, dragging her feet with great reluctance.

I stopped and waited for her to catch up, and said to her in a serious tone, "Your studies are very intense. If you don't pay attention to resting and exercising, your body will suffer, do you understand?"

I continued with earnestness, "You're unwilling to exercise, but do you know I set aside my work just to come down and accompany you?"

After saying this, still seeing Linda’s unwilling face, I got somewhat angry, "**I’m kindly coming down to exercise with you, and you don’t want to. I don’t want to either!**"

At this point, Linda quietly and a bit discontentedly replied, "**Since I don’t want to and you don’t want to, then let’s not exercise.**"

She said this calmly, but it left me utterly **defeated**. I was left speechless and unable to retort, but my heart was fuming. I snapped back, "Go home then, no need to exercise." And she really went back.

I wandered around the neighborhood for a long time, sending my wife many angry messages on WeChat: Raising children is truly pointless... I won’t care about this daughter anymore... How ungrateful and heartless this kid is... I won’t speak to her tonight...

### **The Silent Treatment**

That evening, I stayed alone with my room door closed, eating meals separately and not with them. I ignored all household matters and didn’t say a word of my usually eager reminders to the kids about what to do. Whenever I saw my daughter, I kept a stern face.

This girl, however, acted as if nothing happened, eating and drinking as usual.

The Bible says not to let the sun go down on your anger. But I truly couldn’t manage it.

My wife said, "Shouldn’t you stop giving her the silent treatment? It’s not good for her development."

I said, "Impossible. I need to make her understand that her attitude and words can hurt people and damage relationships. I want to give Linda a lesson so she becomes aware and changes. Besides, I still can’t forgive her deep down."

The next morning, I stayed in my room while they went to school, and in the afternoon when they returned, I remained in the room, ignoring everything at home. I’ve heard of some fathers who "don’t care" about their families, and today, I decided to be that irresponsible, neglectful father. At night, I still ate apart from them, speaking not a single word. Even to my son Anran, I only spoke coldly.

On the third morning, I drove them to school but only served as a driver without speaking a word.

So, for 2 nights and 1 day, I didn’t say a word to Linda. It was the first time the father-daughter relationship became so "tense."

My wife eventually said it couldn’t continue. I agreed; any further coldness would make me a truly excessive father.

My wife helped me analyze why Linda resisted jumping rope that day. Firstly, she felt the pressure and urgency to complete her homework. The fact that she cares so much about her studies is good and worth affirming. Secondly, Linda is apprehensive about jumping rope because during badminton training, the coach assigned the "double jump" exercise, which she tried countless times and never succeeded in, causing strong feelings of defeat.

After hearing this explanation, I had a significant change of heart toward my daughter.

My wife and I discussed arranging time for a private conversation between Linda and me from 4:30 PM, after school, to 5:00 PM, before badminton practice, that Friday afternoon to solve the issue.

### **Reconciliation**

I was really anxious. I asked my wife several times how I should start the conversation and what I should say.

At 4:30, my wife told Linda to find me in the parking lot outside the court where I was waiting inside the car.

Linda opened the back door and got in. I said, "Linda, move to the front passenger seat," (I wanted to have a closer conversation with her).

She moved over.

We sat in silence for a while.

I asked, "Linda, have you been feeling well these past two days?"

She replied, "Not good."

I said, "I’ve also not been feeling good. I haven’t spoken to you for two days."

She asked, "Why does not speaking make you feel bad?"

I explained, "Because not speaking means our relationship is damaged. It’s like I’m not treating you as my child anymore, not as my daughter."

She responded, "Hmm." (Somewhat saddened)

I said, "The day before yesterday, you really made me upset and sad."

(I recounted the events of that day)

I continued, "Do you know why I was sad?"

She said, "Because I didn’t go exercise."

I clarified, "No, you not exercising didn’t make me sad. It was your attitude and words that did. Can you tell me why you didn’t want to exercise that day?"

She admitted, "I don’t like jumping rope because my back hurts when I do it."

I said, "Back pain? You never mentioned that."

She said, "I also don’t like jump rope, I always do it poorly."

I said, "Oh, back pain and not liking jump rope. You can explain that to me. If you say nothing, I can’t know what you’re thinking. Next time, you should explain."

She said, "Dad, I’m sorry. Next time I’ll explain properly."

I said, "I was wrong too. I wasn’t patient with you and got angry easily, and also, I stayed angry and didn’t talk to you for two days. The Bible says not to let the sun go down on your anger, but I didn’t do that. Please forgive me."

Linda started to cry. We talked for about 20 minutes.

I suggested, "Let’s pray together. I’ll start, and then you can pray."

"Heavenly Father, I ask for your forgiveness. I wasn’t patient with Linda and got angry easily. I also found it hard to forgive her quickly, please forgive my sins. Please help Linda and me communicate well in the future and resolve conflicts effectively. I pray in the name of Jesus."

Linda then prayed: "Heavenly Father, please forgive me for my attitude towards Dad that day. I didn’t speak well, nor clearly express my thoughts. Please help me talk to Dad better in the future. In Jesus’ name, I pray."

Finally, I said, "Linda, I want to tell you two things: Firstly, when your attitude and words are inappropriate, they can hurt others, so you need to pay attention to your attitude and words towards others; Secondly, if you have any thoughts, you should express them promptly. If they’re not expressed, it’s hard for others to guess what's on your mind. We've now reconciled, but I believe this won’t be our last conflict. In the future, there will be more, but I hope we can communicate well and quickly reconcile."

Linda agreed, "Okay, Dad."

Then we hugged each other. She was a bit emotional, with tears at the corners of her eyes.

I opened the car door and stepped out. I myself felt relieved and relaxed, and Linda also appeared more at ease. When I spoke to her again, it returned to that relaxed, natural feeling we had before the silent treatment. Because my relationship with Linda had become close again.

### **Reflection**

This silent treatment might make me seem "petty," but it gave me a lot to reflect on.

- Parents can feel "explosive" when met with misunderstanding or rebellion from their children. But my faith in Heavenly Father shows He faces my own rebelliousness with love and patience.
- I’m truly a sinner. I’ve always thought, after over a decade of experience in the workplace, I was invincible in interpersonal relationships. Yet I’ve been defeated by a few light words from my daughter, even those without any intent behind them. Within me, there’s still deep pride and self-righteousness, without humility—I can’t even accept or tolerate my own child.
- Addressing conflicts between parents and children directly might prompt some to say; why bother with kids? Yet misunderstanding, resentment, or even hatred, accumulates bit by bit as the child grows. By the time they reach adulthood, misconceptions and gaps are profound. Parent-child conflicts need to be faced and addressed actively. Restore relationships promptly; the bond between parents and children should be intimate and tight.
- I believe, as Linda grows into her teenage and young adult years, there will inevitably be more differing opinions and conflicts between us. However, from a young age, I hope we can establish a communication style and channel, so even if conflicts arise, we have this channel for communication and tradition, rather than it being closed off.

In the summer of 2006, having worked for a little over a year, I moved alone to Hangzhou. That fall, I accepted an invitation from a friend to "sing and have dinner together on Sundays," which unexpectedly led me to the doors of a Christian church.

It was then that I met Brother Zhou and Sister Sha.

Now, when I see fresh graduates and young professionals at church, especially the young brothers, I am filled with a sense of care. In them, I see my 2006 self—full of ideals, energy, passion, but also naiveté, bewilderment, and ignorance. I often think back to when I was young and the first Christian couple I met—Brother Zhou and Sister Sha—and the influence and help they offered me.

Brother Zhou and Sister Sha weren't much older than me. In 2006, I was 23 while Brother Zhou was probably 28. Reflecting on myself at 28, I didn’t have their maturity, love, and openness to care for the people around me.

Whenever I talk about my journey of faith, I always mention that little church in Hangzhou. Many factors drew me to stay in the church, but one extraordinary reason was Brother Zhou and Sister Sha's kitchen.

At that time, the church gathering was in their home. Sister Sha was a fantastic cook, and I rarely missed a Friday evening. After work on Wensan Road, I would bike to their home, head straight to the kitchen, sit down, and eat (since I often got off work late, they’d usually have eaten by then). I was so comfortable; it felt almost like my own home. Sister Sha would be busy nearby, telling me to eat more. When Brother Zhou had time, he'd sit at the table to discuss news, literature, or joke around, while I focused on eating.

I've half-jokingly told people several times that, in my early years, the reason I stayed with the church was thanks to Sister Sha’s kitchen. Of course, it wasn't just the kitchen; it was also the love they showed because of their Christian faith. Back then, there were several other "single diners" like me.

When a person is single, knowing a well-grounded couple, especially a Christian couple, is a blessing. As singles, we have endless dreams for the future yet remain quite ignorant. How should we live? What kind of friends should we make? What partners should we choose to enter into marriage? During my single years, Brother Zhou and Sister Sha deeply influenced and shaped my understanding in these areas.

By the worldly standards of "having a house, a car, and a substantial bride price," I wasn't ready for marriage at 26.

Seeing Brother Zhou and Sister Sha’s family and experiencing their life up close altered my perceptions. Money is not the sole condition for a happy marriage and family. Even in lack, there can still be laughter, happiness, and care and comfort for those around us. This is the marriage and family life I aspire to. Today, many young singles feel burdened by the high costs of marriage and lose all motivation and hope for marriage due to the unfortunate stories they see and hear.

During my years in Hangzhou, Brother Zhou and Sister Sha always rented their home, and I helped them move two or three times. Each time, Sister Sha made the house clean and homely, filled with the "feel" of Brother Zhou and Sister Sha’s family. They often hosted church brothers and sisters at their home for meals, conversation, and Bible study. Many of these warm scenes remain vivid in my memory. Being with such a family gradually built an inner confidence that you don’t necessarily need to own a home to get married. This has been a significant comfort and encouragement for my life's journey.

Brother Zhou is a knowledgeable, humorous, enthusiastic, and optimistic person. There’s never a dull moment with him. He can talk endlessly about faith, politics, literature, and economics. I was not very articulate, and my knowledge was basic (not much has changed since then). Every time Brother Zhou spoke, I could only listen respectfully and respond simply, yet I learned a lot. I was exposed to many stories and books by scholars and notable figures like Liu Junning, Max Weber, David Livingstone, Hudson Taylor, and John Leighton Stuart. After leaving Hangzhou, I regretted not spending more time conversing and learning from Brother Zhou. As a result, every time we did meet thereafter, I made sure to engage in conversation, soaking up "knowledge energy." By the way, Brother Zhou's specialty dish is scrambled eggs with tomatoes (which I’ve never tasted, only heard about), and his unique skill is "ambidextrous martial arts" 😄 (what kind of skill is that? Those who understand will know).

In 2006, I worked in Hangzhou, got married, and our daughter was born. Seven years later, in 2013, I joined Liulishuo and moved from Hangzhou to Shanghai. Now, in 2020, another seven years have passed. Since leaving Hangzhou, I've seen Brother Zhou and Sister Sha less often, perhaps once or twice a year. Time and distance can indeed dim affection, and love is not always easily expressed. So, I write to commemorate and preserve it.

Thank you, Brother Zhou and Sister Sha. To you, hosting a young person may have been commonplace, but to me, the help and influence you provided are lasting and significant.
